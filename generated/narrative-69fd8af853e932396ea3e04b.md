## Core Views

1. The most important near-term earnings issue is not weak demand, but fuel economics: oil level, kerosene crack spread, Brent-Dubai basis, subsidy level, and the lagged fuel surcharge mechanism.
2. The fuel surcharge does not fully cover the current cost shock because of connecting-flight exceptions and because JAL's hedge basis did not match actual Dubai-linked fuel purchases during the stress window.
3. Domestic and international passenger trends appear healthier than the headline FY2026 plan implies. The planned passenger decline is partly a strategic capacity/yield decision, while China-route reductions have improved supply-demand balance.
4. Cargo is materially above plan in both volume and unit price, helped by long-haul demand and the Cargolux codeshare.
5. Capital allocation is conservative: JAL is still investing through the cycle, while buybacks are paused and debt redemption remains a priority.

## Investment Mechanism

The source implies a two-stage earnings mechanism for [[companies/japan-airlines|JAL]]. In Q1 FY2026, high fuel prices, widened crack spread, and basis mismatch should pressure earnings before surcharge and subsidy offsets catch up. In H2 FY2026, if fuel stabilizes or falls, the lagged fuel surcharge could flip from under-recovery to excess profit while passenger yields, cargo, and domestic business demand remain supportive.

The key variables for the equity case are therefore not only passenger volume and ASK, but also the timing mismatch among fuel purchase cost, hedge effectiveness, government subsidy, and surcharge resets. That creates a potentially sharp expectation gap: investors may read the fuel shock as structurally negative, while management frames it as a temporary Q1/H1 problem with recovery potential if fuel normalizes.

Capital allocation tempers the upside. JAL is not delaying aircraft capex, has annual preferred dividends from subordinated convertible bonds, and faces major subordinated debt redemptions. Even if earnings recover, free cash flow and buyback capacity may remain constrained until the fuel/subsidy environment and redemption schedule become clearer.

## Expectation Gap

The market risk is that investors focus on the headline FY2026 fuel burden and planned passenger decline. The source suggests a more nuanced read: domestic and international demand are currently exceeding plan, the China-route impact has largely resolved through capacity discipline and fare hikes, and cargo is significantly above plan.

The upside gap is H2 recovery from surcharge lag plus cargo strength. The downside gap is that basis spread, crack spread, and connecting-flight surcharge exceptions reveal the fuel hedge and surcharge system is less complete than a simple sensitivity table would imply.

## Investment Implications

For [[companies/japan-airlines|JAL]], the source is neutral-to-constructive on underlying demand but cautious on near-term earnings quality. It supports monitoring the stock through a fuel/subsidy/cargo framework rather than a simple passenger-volume framework.

Important triggers are: the May 2026 subsidy conclusion, the May 22 domestic aviation expert panel, Q1 FY2026 fuel cost realization, the July 2026 fuel surcharge effect, cargo yield persistence, and whether management restarts buybacks after visibility improves.

For [[industries/airlines|Airlines]], this source reinforces that 2026 earnings dispersion may come from fuel basis, surcharge mechanics, route mix, and cargo/loyalty monetization, not only passenger recovery.
