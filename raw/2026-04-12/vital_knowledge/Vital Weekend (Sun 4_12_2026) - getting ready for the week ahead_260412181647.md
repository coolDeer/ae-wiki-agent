US/Iran meeting in Pakistan: bull vs. bear takeaways
- What bulls are saying – the fact the meeting took place at all is a sign of progress . The highest-level contact between US and Iranian officials in nearly half a century occurred just days after a brutal war, and talks stretched on for nearly 24 hours . Trump Sunday morning wrote that “ the meeting went well ” and that “ most points were agreed to ”. The last major deal between the US and Iran, the Obama-era nuclear agreement, took two years to reach , so to think a full accord could be struck after just a weekend was unrealistic (it seems like the US and Iran reached agreement on certain topics during the weekend negotiations, and a Reuters report made it sound like Washington was prepared to release billions of frozen Iranian funds ). The current ceasefire (which continues to hold) still has more time (it ends on Apr 21), mediators (especially Pakistan) are working furiously behind the scenes to sustain the détente, and communications are ongoing between the two sides (a spokesman for Iran’s foreign ministry released the following statement: “we should never have expected to reach a deal in one session. We will continue to work to bring the two views of Americans and Iranians closer together”. A diplomat quoted in the FT said more negotiations were expected , but nothing had been scheduled). While the war in Lebanon is ongoing, Israel has dialed back the intensity of its strikes and seems to be observing a ceasefire in/around Beirut (officials from Israel, Lebanon, and the US are set to meet this week ). Two US Navy warships entered Hormuz over the weekend for the first time since the war commenced as part of an initiative to sweep for mines , and Saudi Arabia said it has fully restored its East-West pipeline (which bypassed Hormuz) to ~7M BPD. Trump’s options for escalation in Iran remain awful (which is why he’s unlikely to take them): destroying Iran’s civilian infrastructure , militarily (i.e. with troops) reopening Hormuz (which would involve putting tens, if not hundreds of thousands, of soldiers on the ground for an extended period), and/or seizing Iran’s enriched uranium (which could be even more complex than using troops to guarantee Hormuz passage). His decision to impose a blockade of Hormuz (announced Sunday morning) isn’t ideal , but it’s better than a dramatic escalation in the military campaign .
- What bears are saying – the talks in Pakistan broke down over stark disagreements over critical and fundamental issues , including Hormuz control, the existing stockpile of enriched uranium, and Iran’s right to enrich. Vance said the US outlined its “best and final” offer , suggesting Washington wasn’t interested in negotiating further (either Iran can take what’s on the table or not), and Netanyahu told the Israeli public over the weekend that “ the battle is not yet over ” regarding Iran . A trio of WSJ articles published over the weekend suggested the recent military campaign against Iran largely failed to achieve its key objectives ( these reports, if accurate, are even more negative than the lack of a deal in Pakistan ): “ The U.S. Sank One of Iran’s Navies. The Other Still Controls Hormuz ” ( WSJ ), “ Iran Has Thousands of Missiles and Could Retrieve Launchers, U.S. Intelligence Finds ” ( WSJ ), and “ Iran’s Nuclear Program Has Survived, Clouding Talks ” ( WSJ ). Iran’s regime remains firmly entrenched , and the country still possesses a capability and will to wage a campaign of economic warfare against critical assets in the region (the damage inflicted to key energy infrastructure in Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and elsewhere, is already substantial ). Tehran doesn’t need to expend much energy to keep Hormuz closed , and ships will avoid passing until Iran firmly commits to freedom of navigation (a NYT report over the weekend claimed that one of the big problems is that Iran can’t locate all the mines it laid during the war ). Trump’s decision to impose a Hormuz blockade risks triggering escalation (Trump warned that “ any Iranian who fires on us, or at peaceful vessels, will be blown to hell ”).
- How we’re thinking about the situation – the Pakistan meeting was definitely a disappointment for US equities (especially considering the recent rally and the fact the SPX had rebounded back to the pre-war resistance zone of ~6900-7000), but we don’t think it will trigger an immediate collapse of the ceasefire (which still seems to be holding as of Sunday morning) or an escalation in fighting (our core view remains that the escalatory options available to Trump are so bad that they remain unlikely, which is why he decided to impose a Hormuz blockade rather than launch a fresh round of strikes and/or a ground troop deployment ). Agreement was apparently reached on certain points (which Trump acknowledged Sunday morning – “ the meeting went well...most points were agreed to ”), and neither side is ruling out further talks . Iran has made commitments in the recent past not to pursue a nuclear weapon (this was a cornerstone of the 2015 nuclear agreement) and it wouldn’t take much for it to do so again , while its enriched uranium seems largely inaccessible . The IDF seems to be observing a ceasefire in/around Beirut , and it’s possible the talks between Israel, Lebanon, and the US this week (scheduled to occur in Washington) could yield further progress (which could help the US-Iran relationship ).

Data/monetary policy recap from Friday morning
- What bulls were talking about : the Michigan sentiment report was bad , but 1) 98% of the interviews took place before the recent ceasefire announcement and 2) long-term inflation expectations (which the Fed cares about most) were fairly muted. The Mar CPI wasn’t as bad as feared , with the core CPI only accelerating 10bp to +2.6% (vs. the Street +2.7%).
- What bears were talking about : the US CPI for Mar wasn’t as bad as feared, but inflation is still running very hot at +3.3% headline and +2.6% core (and the core number will face further upside pressure in the months ahead as it will take some time for the recent energy price spike to seep further into the broader economy). The Michigan sentiment survey for Apr was net negative , with a sharp deterioration in Expectations (down >5 points M/M to 46.1) and a big short-term inflation jump (1-year inflation expectations rose 100bp to +4.8% vs. +3.8% in Mar). The inability of Treasuries to rally at all this week is a giant red flag about the inflationary implications of the war. San Francisco Fed President Daly warned in a Reuters interview that the Iran war-driven oil shock will make it even harder for US inflation to return to the 2% target (“We had work to do before we had the oil price shock; with the oil price shock, the work just takes longer”). China’s PPI had been a major source of global disinflation , but those days might be coming to an end based on the Mar data (the PPI flipped into positive territory for the first time in more than three years).
