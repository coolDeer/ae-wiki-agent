As discussed in last week’s scuttlebit, Danaher’s life sciences coverage can basically be boiled down to 3 types of products: 1) diagnostic instruments and assays that hospitals and labs use to identify diseases, 2) research instruments and consumables that scientists use to study those diseases and discover drugs to fight them, and 3) tools and equipment that CDMOs and biopharmas use to manufacture those drugs. Thermo Fisher, nearly twice Danaher’s size by revenue, envelopes even more of the life sciences space. Whereas Danaher limits itself to products, Thermo Fisher provides services as well, running clinical trials on behalf of biopharma clients and manufacturing drugs for them too.
Here’s a run-through of what’s been happening in each of Thermo’s division over the last several years:
Life Sciences
![image](https://www.scuttleblurb.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/tmoa1.webp)

The Life Sciences segment, created through the $15bn acquisition of Life Technologies in 2014, is made up of 3 business units: Biosciences, Genetic Sciences, and Bioproduction.
Thermo’s bioproduction business is much smaller than Danaher’s in both absolute terms (~$4bn1 vs. $6bn) and as a percentage of total revenue (9% vs. 25%). It boasts a strong position in cell culture media (nutrients that keeps cells alive and productive in the bioreactor) and single-use technologies (disposable bags and tubes), and maintains a decent, though not leading, presence in purification (chromatography resins and columns that isolate proteins of interest). Last year’s $4bn acquisition of Solventum’s Purification and Filtration division plugs the gap between those two steps, adding filtration technologies that remove cells and debris from the harvest before purification begins.
The other 2 sub-segments of Life Sciences – Biosciences and Genetic Sciences – mostly serve research and drug discovery, though both have some clinical diagnostic uses as well (for instance, during the pandemic, hospitals and clinics used PCR assays to test for the presence of SARS-CoV-2 RNA). Biosciences houses tools and reagents used to tag and sort cells, measure proteins, and prepare samples for experiments. Genetic Sciences covers instruments and assays that analyze DNA and RNA, rather than the proteins they encode, allowing researchers to measure how treatments alter gene expression in cells.
Life Sciences surged during the pandemic, with revenue more than doubling between 2019 and 2021 on the back of booming demand for PCR assays and for bioproduction products to manufacture COVID vaccines. Revenue then fell precipitously as COVID diagnostic demand collapsed and bioproduction channel inventory was depleted.
Bioproduction demand has since recuperated industrywide. Danaher and Sartorius reported high single digit organic growth in 2025. Thermo doesn’t separately disclose organic bioproduction growth but characterizes it as “excellent” (backing out 4 months of Solventum in 2025, we might infer 11% organic growth). Its recovery, however, has been offset by what appears to be contraction across the remaining ~60% of Life Sciences – the part most exposed to early-stage research funding – blending division-wide organic growth down to just 3%.
Analytical Instruments
![image](https://www.scuttleblurb.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/tmoa2.webp)

Analytical Instruments is anchored by chromatographers (separates molecules with different physical properties) and mass spectrometers (identifies particular molecules based on differences in mass/charge ratios), which together account ~45% of segment revenue. Electron microscopes (uses electrons, rather than light, to resolve images down to the atomic level) make up another ~40%.
Though it sells expensive products for early-stage drug discovery, Analytical Instruments has managed to avoid the y/y declines reported by Danaher’s equipment-heavy Life Sciences segment over the past 2 years, which management attributes in large part to its equipment being used in advanced research, where scientists generally want the most cutting edge stuff. Biopharma organizations will purchase instruments outside their normal upgrade cycles if doing so can help accelerate research programs, which helps offset sudden pullbacks from academic labs that depend on grant funding. Thermo’s instrument portfolio also has an excellent reputation at the high-end of the market. In advanced proteomics workflows, for instance, the Orbitrap brand of mass spectrometers is universally praised.
![image](https://www.scuttleblurb.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/tmoa3.webp)

Specialty Diagnostics
![image](https://www.scuttleblurb.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/tmoa4.webp)

Specialty Diagnostics, the smallest and lowest-margin of Thermo’s divisions, sells instruments and assays targeting high-value niches like allergies and autoimmunity, where it holds leading positions, transplant donor-recipient matching and, through its $2.7bn acquisition of Binding Site in 2023, multiple myeloma testing.
It avoids the commoditized high-volume testing that Danaher addresses through Beckman Coulter and is about half the size of Danaher Diagnostics by revenue. But the macro challenges it’s faced over the last few years are pretty similar. Lingering post-COVID testing headwinds in 2024 were more than offset by good results in transplant matching and immunodiagnostics. That strength carried into 2025 but was tempered by the same value-based procurement challenges in China that Danaher contended with.
![image](https://www.scuttleblurb.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/tmoa5.webp)

It is a bit curious that in 2022, Thermo’s Diagnostics revenue decline 13% as the COVID tailwind dissipated while Danaher’s grew 13.5%. As far as I can tell, Danaher’s relative success had mostly to do with colossal results at Cepheid, whose revenue swelled by about 30% that year as the respiratory portion fared much better than hoped on the strength of its 4-in-1 combination test. In the last three quarters of 2022, management expected $1.1bn of respiratory revenue. Instead, Cepheid delivered $2.7bn, about what it generated in all of 2021.
Whether due to sharper execution or greater exposure to the right disease categories, Danaher’s Diagnostics business has left Thermo’s in the dust, with revenue and profits growing more than 2x and 3x as fast, respectively, since 2018, even absent the acquisition boost that Thermo enjoyed2. Both companies argued that the COVID-fueled expansion in instrument sales would support a much larger base of recurring revenue going forward, as testing for other infectious diseases replaced COVID across a significantly expanded installed base. But only Danaher appears to have made good on that promise.
![image](https://www.scuttleblurb.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/tmoa6.webp)

Lab products and biopharma services
![image](https://www.scuttleblurb.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/04/tmoa7.webp)

The largest of Thermo’s segments, representing more than half of total revenue, Laboratory Products and Biopharma Services smooshes together four business lines.
Lab products makes and sells basic lab supplies, equipment, and chemicals used in research and drug development….things like pipettes, tubes, vials, beakers, flasks, chemicals, reagents, incubators, freezers. Research and safety market channel, better known by its trade name, Fisher Scientific, is Thermo’s distribution and e-commerce arm, widely regarded as the Amazon of the life sciences industry for its breadth of product offerings. Through it, Thermo sells 2.5 million SKUs, both its own products and those of more than 9k third-party suppliers. In some cases, it manages customer stockrooms directly, ensuring a constant flow of critical supplies for lab managers. This is a mature but steady business with relatively low margins that competes on scale, logistics, and service.
Much more emphasis is placed on the division’s newer and more dynamic services portion, where Thermo manages clinical trials as a CRO (namely, through PPD) and manufactures drugs for biopharma clients as a CDMO (Patheon + Branner Bio).
Many were understandably baffled when Thermo announced the $21bn acquisition of PPD in 2021, wondering what business a life sciences products vendor had running clinical research trials, never mind the hefty 18x fully-synergized EBITDA multiple it shelled out to do so. The strategic merits remain puzzling. Thermo appears to have managed PPD well as a standalone unit, growing its revenue by ~8%/year, from ~$5.9bn to ~$8bn. Most of that strength, however, has been front-end loaded, with revenue growing by just lsd in 2024, then barely at all for most of last year – $200mn of studies, specifically those pertaining to vaccines, were canceled or put on hold – before finishing 4q up mid-single digits. We are told that “very, very strong” authorizations augur rosier growth in the years ahead.
Thermo’s CDMO business is the product of 3 acquisitions: 1) Patheon ($7.4bn; Aug. ‘17, 15x synergized revenue), which manufactures small and large molecule drugs and holds a leading position in the fill-finish phase, where sterile biologics are injected into vials and syringes; and 2) Brammer Bio ($1.7bn; April ‘19; 7x revenue) and Henogen ($830mn; January ‘21), which specialize more narrowly in the production of viral vectors, the engineered viruses that carry therapeutic genetic material into a patient’s cells.
The latter two targets seem to have fared poorly. Brammer laid off hundreds of employees and consolidated its plant footprint in 2023 and 2024. Also, Thermo’s management team tends to play up every green shoot, so the fact that they barely talk about gene therapy anymore and haven’t mentioned Brammer Bio by name in literally more than 2 years is a sign that things are not going well at all. It’s fair to say that Brammer has fallen egregiously short of the 25%+ growth that management projected at the time it was acquired. Of course, Thermo is hardly alone in this regard. The promise of gene therapy, which drew so much excitement and capital in the immediate aftermath of COVID, has so far been confounded by high costs, limited patient access, and regulatory caution.
But Patheon, which at the time of its acquisition reported nearly 8x the revenue of Brammer, is the far more significant driver of value and here, supply/demand dynamics appear much healthier. Revenue has grown from $1.9bn at the time of its acquisition in 2017 to ~$7bn in 2024 (I think that $7bn includes Brammer and Henogen, which were doing ~$350mn of revenue when they were acquired. Impressive nonetheless).
With trade tensions rising, many biopharma companies are expected to shift more manufacturing activity back to the US. Thermo stands to benefit from this in two ways. On the product side, like Danaher, it should see growing demand for bioprocessing equipment as biopharmas and CDMOs build or expand domestic manufacturing facilities. On the services side, biopharmas may choose to outsource production to Thermo’s domestically owned manufacturing plants. When Novo Nordisk acquired Catalent last year, it effectively yanked capacity from a fill-finish market that was already capacity-constrained. Thermo Fisher has stepped into this void, adding manufacturing lines and recently purchasing Sanofi’s fill-finish facility in New Jersey.
From time to time, observers have pointed out that in buying Patheon, Thermo created a conflict of interest in that it now competes with the very CDMOs it sells bioprocessing tools to. Maybe, but I really can’t point to any signs that this has been a make-or-break factor for clients.
Thermo is not so much a centrally controlled life sciences empire as much as it is a collection of assets that function more or less independently. Cross-subsidization is narrowly scoped within platforms or product domains. A client might enjoy volume-based discounts when they centralize orders for beakers, flasks, and freezers or combine purchases of antibody conjugates and flow cytometers. But you’re not going to see Thermo offering steep discounts on, like, mass spectrometers in return for more bioproduction tubing sales or whatever because the divisions that sell those products operate as distinct silos optimizing their own P&Ls. Patheon, which operates under a separate brand, enjoys the same volume-based discounts on Thermo bioproduction gear as any other client of comparable scale, but it isn’t buying at cost. Indeed, such cross-subsidization across divisions would fundamentally undermine Thermo’s carefully cultivated positioning as a trusted partner. Management frequently emphasizes that even during the supply-constrained pandemic period, it consistently supported all customers rather than prioritizing its own internal programs.
I think Thermo’s value proposition as an end-to-end provider for biotech and pharmas, who comprise ~60% of revenue3 – “enabling the early research to a scientific idea through the designing of trials, executing the trials, developing the medicines, giving them the technologies to produce the medicines or producing it for them in-house” – has less to do with bundling economics than with the convenience of streamlining procurement through a single supplier, which by shortening development timelines can reduce the risk of losing the race to market.
The most recent and salient example of this Thermo’s Accelerator Drug Development solution, which combines the company’s CRO and CDMO capabilities. An integrated offering makes a lot of sense. Patheon (and CDMOs generally) manufacture drugs for clinical trials in addition to drugs that have been approved. Also, clinical trials and drug manufacturing are directly adjacent and overlapping phases in the drug development lifecycle, so outsourcing to a single entity that does both should ease handoff frictions. ADD was in the works for years but wasn’t publicly announced until last January, after Thermo had amassed enough proof points of success. Marc is generous with superlatives – the impact has been “really compelling”, customer feedback “incredibly positive”, the speed and efficiency gains “a huge value-add”, and adoption, especially among biotechs, has been “very strong”.
Stepping back from the individual segments, there are several high level points worth considering.
First, like Danaher, Thermo is wrestling with dual headwinds from China (8% of revenue) and weakness in academic and government markets (15% of total revenue, with US-specific A&G about half of that). These two issues – China and US A&G – have absorbed inordinate attention on earnings calls this year, as near-term macro issues tend to do. This too, shall pass. Thermo will adapt and manage through these events – tariffs, China, re-shoring, government shutdowns, whatever – with adjustments to its supply chain and pricing, and I bet in five years we’ll wonder what all the fuss was about. The more relevant secular drivers – an aging population, the shift toward biologics, the indefatigable human spirit driving ever more scientific advances – all remain in tact.
Second, as you can probably guess, AI is being squeezed into every crevice. Alongside generic enterprise-level use cases – marketing, software development, customer support, inventory management, product recommendations in its e-commerce sites – Thermo is finding ways to leverage AI across its products and services. In clinical research, it recently announced a collaboration with OpenAI to shorten trial times and expects the partnership to expand to other areas over time. We can also expect Thermo and its peers to embed AI in the data analytics and diagnostic software accompanying their instruments, as well as use the data exhaust from their install bases to train and refine those algorithms over time.
In my Bio-Techne series, I speculated on various ways AI could be leveraged across various phases of the development lifecycle:
From a vast universe of molecules, AI can be used to identify those most likely to attach to a protein’s most important binding pockets. Those candidates can then be tested in vivo to see whether they have the desired effect, with the results used to iteratively fine tune the model. In the last few years, I’ve become aware of a number of startups – Atomwise, Insilico Medicine, Recursion Pharmceuticals, Benevolent AI, among others – that leverage AI to discover novel drug…
Clinical and pre-clinical CROs are adopting AI to streamline workflows – for instance, IQVIA’s algorithms can surface problems with trial design and sort through health records to identify promising recruits. But ultimately, digital efficiency gains run up against the speed limits of biology and regulatory processes….
It’s also possible that the union of AI-unlocked drug discoveries, workflow efficiencies, and CGT approvals unleashes a golden age of personalized medicine that in turn triggers a groundswell of demand for assays required to characterize a growing variety of molecules, with those assays in turn fueling demand for more advanced diagnostic instruments. But again, this argument relies on the relative pace of de-bottlenecking elsewhere in the drug development process…
Should AI accelerate the pace of drug discovery and development, Thermo-Fisher and Danaher both stand to benefit.
Third, compared to Danaher, who blew its post-COVID M&A load on two huge acquisitions – Aldevron (a CDMO specializing in the production of nucleic acids used as inputs for mRNA vaccines and viral vectors used in gene therapies) and Abcam (proteins and antibodies used in drug discovery) – Thermo’s deals have been smaller and spread across a few more targets. After setting a foundation in CRO with the mammoth acquisition of PPD, management then purchased PeproTech ($1.9bn; Jan. ‘22), which competes with Abcam in selling recombinant proteins for drug research; Binding Site ($2.7bn, Jan. ‘23), which develops assays and instruments to diagnose blood cancers and immune-system disorders in clinical labs; and Olink ($3.1bn; Jul. ‘24), whose antibodies are used to detect protein biomarkers, mostly in research settings4.
Thermo built a strong proteomics franchise through these three acquisitions. But it paid up the wazoo too. PeproTech was purchased for 19x revenue, Binding Site for 11x. Olink was taken out at 16x revenue, or 25x year 5 operating profit. Last September, Thermo scooped up Solventum’s filtration business for $4bn, valuing the target, which should grow “around or above the company average”, at 41x EBIT, more like 18x if you give full credit for revenue and cost synergies that won’t be fully realized until year 5. A month later, it announced the $8.9bn acquisition of Clario ($9.4bn with full earn-outs) at a more reasonable ~21x EBITDA.
Clario basically sells software and devices that CROs and biopharmas use to collect, standardize, and interpret patient-reported symptoms, imaging, heart-safety data, and other specialized endpoints produced during clinical trials. It overlaps with Veeva in a few spots, notably eCOA (questionnaire responses from patients and clinicians) but is more of a measurement vendor for studies – specialized tools and software that measure specific trial outcomes and safety signals – whereas Veeva is better thought of as the operational and data-management backbone. Strategically, the deal makes sense. Thermo already runs clinical trials through PPD, so owning the systems that capture endpoint metrics is a logical adjacency. It can offer trial sponsors an integrated platform for running clinical trials and analyzing the data spilling off of them.
Based on the difference between the $1.1bn of revenue disclosed in this June 2025 Bloomberg article and the $1.25bn of revenue that it did last year, Clario looks to be growing ~9%/year? If we assume operating earnings grow by 10% and tack on $175mn of expected cost synergies, I can get to a year-5 ROIC of around 9%5, which isn’t amazing but better than the returns that Thermo will likely realize on other recent deals. High-single digit year-5 ROICs are about the best we can expect for targets that are large enough to move the needle for someone of Thermo’s size.
Thermo’s widely admired CEO, Marc Casper, has a habit of putting a positive spin on things. It’s like his range of negative-to-positive valence has been shifted about two notches above the average. You say “double digits”, Marc says “strong teens”. He describes the subsequent performance of acquired targets with characteristic pep – Binding Site is “crushing it”, “growing incredibly strong” with “super happy customers, great innovation”’; Olink customers are “totally excited about what we’re doing across the world”. He paints with broad sweeps of qualitative color and leaves us guessing about the details. We don’t know how much profit Thermo is realizing on the $7.7bn spent on Olink, Binding Site, and Peptrotech. And even with its $4bn price tag, Solventum Filtration was yawningly characterized as a “tuck-in”, its “mid-teens” margins divulged once in passing.
Thermo has produced ~11% pre-tax returns on incremental capital since 2019 (excluding Solventum in the 2025 denominator). But this doesn’t tell us the extent to which acquisitions, which have absorbed roughly 70% of the company’s free cash flow since 2018, were accretive or dilutive to those returns. It could be that most of the incremental profit between 2018 and today came from the Thermo’s business as it existed in 2018. If we assume Thermo’s business as of year-end 2018 grew profits by 6%/year, then we’d be at ~$9.2bn EBITDA compared to the ~$11.2bn that Thermo actually generated in 2025, implying rather meager returns on the ~$32bn spent on acquisitions (again, ex. Solventum) in the interim.
Most, or possibly all, of this gap – between the $11.2bn and $9.2bn – can be plugged by PPD, which at the time of acquisition in 2021 generated $1.2bn of EBITDA (including synergies) on a purchase price of ~$21bn, which then suggests pitifully little has been earned on the remaining ~$11bn. That this management team, prone to highlighting every inkling of positive news, has never called out the profitability of any post-2018 acquisition except PPD only reinforces this suspicion.
To be clear, none of this is to say that Thermo has destroyed value through M&A over its broader history. On the contrary, its pre-2019 acquisitions, done at reasonable low-teens multiples of EBITDA, appear to have worked out quite well on the whole. FEI, the foundation of Thermo’s electron microscopy business, did $973mn of revenue when it was acquired in 2016 for 14x EBITDA and generates more like $3bn today. Patheon, acquired for 15x EBITDA in 2017, has ~tripled its revenue.
As for organic performance, management expects 7% to 9% revenue growth, a function of 4% to 6% industry growth plus a few points from share gains, with 40 to 50 basis points of annual margin expansion producing 10% annual growth in operating income. Maybe, let’s see. The secular themes remain promising, but keep in mind that Thermo’s historical performance – 6% organic growth and flattish margins over the past decade – has fallen a bit short of this target6. Thermo has indeed grown EBITDA by ~10%/year during this period, but doing so required spending nearly $50bn on acquisitions!
