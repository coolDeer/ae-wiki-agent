Following the Llama 4 fiasco and almost 9 months after rebuilding the Meta Superintelligence team (MSL), Meta has finally released a new model: Muse Spark ! Just a month ago, The New York Times depicted a pretty unflattering picture of how Meta’s efforts in developing the frontier model are not going in the right direction and claimed Meta would delay the release of the model to May. Meta was a month early of that timeline which was a positive surprise for investors. Perhaps the more welcome surprise is that this actually appears to be a more than a decent model. In Arena’s (formerly LMArena) leaderboard , Muse Spark currently lags behind just Claude 4.6 in text and vision although it still lags materially in some areas such as coding. Nonetheless, given Meta AI’s target consumer market, coding may not be the use case they need to be best at.

Of course, there are legitimate concerns related to “Benchmaxxing” which means the most credible sign of Meta being back in model race may actually come from app store ranking. As of this writing, Meta AI is actually ahead of all other AI apps on iOS App Store.
Zuckerberg has been making the case for a while that once the underlying quality of Meta’s model improves, it will lead to higher usage and engagement. From 3Q’25 call:
> “what we see is that as we improve the quality of the model, primarily for post-training Llama 4 at this point. We are -- we continue to see improvements in usage. So our view is that when we get the new models that we’re building in MSL in there and get like truly frontier models with novel capabilities that you don’t have in other places, then I think that this is just a massive latent opportunity”

I don’t think Meta has unleashed any novel capability with Muse Spark yet. I actually don’t think they need to get such capability for increasing adoption of Meta AI. Ultimately, what Meta (and Google) needs is to offer a noticeably better model than what ChatGPT or Claude offers for their free users. Hence, I think it is not even of paramount importance for Meta (and Google) to have the most frontier model for competing in consumer AI use cases; they just need to comfortably beat the model and product experiences offered to free users of ChatGPT. The Information reported that OpenAI is projecting $102 Billion ad revenue by 2030 which implies that they will have Weekly Active Users (WAU) of 2.75 Billion by then. Color me skeptical, especially on the WAU front!
OpenAI’s easiest period for customer acquisition is actually behind them. Since there is no noticeable network effects, it is highly likely that the next 500 million WAU will be much more expensive to acquire than the last 500 million. They will have to do so while Google and Meta will likely surpass the underlying model quality and rate limits that ChatGPT can offer for free users. That is an uphill task, especially in a compute constrained environment when OpenAI is simultaneously competing for the potentially highly lucrative enterprise dollars against Anthropic! The fact that ChatGPT’s MAU in the US appears to be essentially flat for the last six months is likely a strong indication that it has already been a pretty uphill task for ChatGPT to maintain WAU growth in the face of competition from Gemini 3.0 (and later Claude). If Meta AI can keep their shipping cadence for the rest of this year, that will make a massive dent in OpenAI’s ambitions in growing WAU.

Google may still need to compete against Anthropic and OpenAI much more fiercely in enterprise markets. Meta has no such “baggage”. At least theoretically, Meta actually is incredibly well positioned to stay extremely focused on nailing the consumer use cases in AI. While many seem to be wondering whether consumer AI is nearly as appealing as enterprise AI, I do want to highlight again that the opportunity in consumer AI is also pretty substantial as long as you have the userbase and attention:
> “Let’s imagine Meta is able to get 1 billion DAU on “Meta AI” in three years (~25% of their Daily Active People on their properties). Let’s say the average DAU on Meta AI asks 360 queries per year. If 20% of these queries have commercial intent, that’s 72 high-intent queries for which Meta can show ads to users later. If ~10% of these high-intent ads convert and each conversion is worth $10-20, that’s ~$70-140 Billion incremental revenue opportunity for Meta. Remember, an average Meta DAU likely sees more than 30k ads on Meta per year. Even if there is some “cannibalization” here (perhaps Meta would have been able to show some of these ads without the help from “Meta AI” anyway), you can still sense that the incremental opportunity can become quite large for incumbent companies with existing userbase and SOTA advertising infrastructure.”

A couple of weeks ago, Meta published a blog post highlighting their new adaptive ranking model which I thought was quite revealing in how they have been using AI to become even more effective in advertising.
Historically, ad networks ran every potential ad through the same rigid prediction model to see which one you were most likely to click. As models became smarter (approaching the trillion-parameter scale), they also became vastly heavier and slower. Applying an LLM-scale model to thousands of potential ads for every single user scroll is physically impossible if you want the app to load instantly without bankrupting the company on GPU costs. The Adaptive Ranking Model solves this by abandoning the “one-size-fits-all” approach. It operates on two main principles:
Intelligent Routing: Instead of forcing every ad request through the heaviest model, the system quickly assesses the user’s context. If the intent is simple and obvious, it routes the request to a lighter, faster model. If the user’s behavioral signals are complex, it routes the request to the heavy, LLM-scale model.
Compute-Once, Use-Many (Request-Oriented Optimization): In the old paradigm, if Meta had 1,000 potential ads to show you, it would independently calculate your user profile against Ad 1, then Ad 2, all the way to Ad 1,000. This is linear scaling, and it creates massive redundancy. The new model computes your deep, heavy behavioral sequence exactly once per request, holds it in the GPU memory, and broadcasts it across all 1,000 ad candidates simultaneously.
![image](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!w-26!,w_2400,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1ff973b6-8afd-4a5c-9124-94cdc573e99b_1920x1223.png)
To help you understand the difference between old and new approach a bit better, let me explain with an example. Imagine you walk into a huge department store and a personal shopper has to pick the 10 best items to show you out of 1,000 possibilities. The shopper grabs item #1 off the shelf, then turns to you and asks: “ Who are you? What do you like? What did you buy recently? What mood are you in? What’s the weather? What did you browse last week? ” They think hard about you, then think about whether item #1 is a fit, and give it a score. Then they put item #1 down, grab item #2, turn to you, and ask the exact same questions all over again : “Who are you, what do you like, what did you buy recently…”
They do this 1,000 times. Every single item gets a fresh interview. By the time they’ve scored everything, they’ve asked you the same questions a thousand times and done the same thinking about you a thousand times. Most of that work was identical and wasted as you didn’t change between item #1 and item #847.
This is roughly how traditional ad ranking works. For each candidate ad, the model rebuilds its understanding of the user from scratch . If there are 1,000 ads to score, the user-side computation runs 1,000 times.
The “adaptive ranking model” approaches this very differently. The shopper turns to you once at the start. They take a long, careful look…really studies you, asks about your week, your mood, your style, your recent purchases. They build a rich mental picture of you and write it down on a notepad.
Then they walk through the 1,000 items, and for each one they just glance at the item and check it against the notepad. “ Does this match what I already know about her? Yes/no, score it. ” The expensive part i.e. understanding you happened just once and the per-item scoring part is fast and cheap.
This is Meta’s request-centric architecture. The “who is this user” computation happens one time per request, the result gets shared across all candidate ads, and only the lightweight “does this specific ad fit?” check runs per ad.
In the old way, if you want a smarter shopper i.e. one who notices subtler things about you, every one of those 1,000 interviews gets longer. Doubling the shopper’s intelligence roughly doubled the total work. Since costs scale linearly with model size in the old approach, nobody could afford to make these models much bigger.
In the new way, making the shopper smarter only makes the one upfront interview longer. The 1,000 quick item-checks can stay cheap. So you can give the shopper a vastly bigger “brain” (almost the size of a frontier language model i.e. 1 trillion parameter) without the total cost exploding.
Meta’s system also decides how much shopper intelligence to deploy per visitor. A casual browser who’s clearly just killing time gets a lighter-weight shopper. A high-intent visitor who looks ready to buy something expensive gets the full trillion-parameter genius so that compute gets spent where it actually moves the needle. This is basically the core idea of adaptive ranking model: understand the person once, score the ads cheaply against that understanding, and spend your biggest brain only where it pays off.
This new approach has already started paying off. Meta mentioned in their blog post (emphasis mine):
> “Adaptive Ranking Model delivers a significant increase in ad conversions and advertiser value while maintaining system-wide computational efficiency. This ensures superior performance for businesses of all sizes. Since launching on Instagram in Q4 2025, Adaptive Ranking Model has delivered a +3% increase in ad conversions and +5% increase in ad click through rate for targeted users.”

Of course, Adaptive Ranking Model is just one of the many optimizations Meta is running in its advertising infrastructure. It is worth noting this is one of three Meta ads ML posts in the last six months: GEM (the “central brain” generative ads model) in November 2025, Ranking Engineer Agent in March 2026, and now Adaptive Ranking Model . You add all of these optimizations together and it all starts to make sense why their business has been accelerating in the recent quarters even if the business is getting larger.
While Meta is set to face a summer of lawsuits which will likely lead to a barrage of negative headlines in the coming months (with no definitive outcome in the near future given these may all go to the Supreme Court), the core business does seem extremely well positioned to be one of the largest beneficiaries of AI. If they can also build the best model for consumer AI use cases, that will be icing on the cake!
