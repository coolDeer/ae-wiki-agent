# Coronavirus impacts on smartphones

The breakout of 2019-nCoV has been faster than expected, creating supply disruptions due logistical challenges. This leads to the production hiccups, as China plays a vital role in global smartphone supply chain. Same time, phycological impacts – uncertainty and anxiety could lead to a softer demand in the near term. From the supply angle, Samsung’s supply chain might be less impacted as most of its productions are based in Vietnam and Korea. As for iPhone, while we believe demand for iPhone 11 and AirPod Pro is likely to stay solid, a prolong delays of resumption of productions could lead to uncertainty in supply. While we expect many smartphone OEMs and vendors to report weaker than expected 1Q or even 2Q results, should nCoV be contained, the supply chain could see a V-shape rebound in 2H.

# The impact on the supply chain – disruption is inevitable…

China is the global manufacturing hub of technology and electronic products. For example, Hubei province is the manufacturing hub of China’s optic communication industry. Zhengzhou is the main production site for iPhone. Shenzhen is the hub of various smartphone components, as are Guangzhou, Wenzhou, and others. There have been reports of transportation and logistical disruptions in these areas.

We believe the production hiccup is inevitable, given China plays a vital role in smartphone supply chain. The smartphone EMS and mechanical component industries are basically dominated by Chinese firms or companies that have mass production site in China (such as Hon Hai, Pegatron, and Catcher). We estimate China accounts for \~80% of global smartphone production. In the component level, we estimate 80%+ of touch panels, plastic casings, metal frames, USB connectors/cables, battery package, and acoustics are made in China. In addition, 70%+ of camera modules, LCD display, and standardized passive components (MLCC, inductor, and resister) are made in China too.

# …doesn’t Hon Hai just reiterate that they can resume production on Feb 10?

Yes, but this is a vague guideline. Based on our knowledge, Hon Hai never fully shut down the operation of key production sites (such as those in Zhengzhou, Shenzhen and a number of other cities) during the Chinese New Year period. A small portion of workers opted to stay (and will get extra pay) and kept the production up and running till now. We believe most of them will continue to work in coming weeks, as long as there is no infected case reported among them. So, technically speaking, yes, Hon Hai’s factories will be still up and running after Feb 10.

However, due to the transportation and logistical disruptions, it is getting challenging for tech firms to recruit enough work force to bring the operation back to full capacity. In addition, even if Hon Hai can resolve the labour shortage issue, we believe it will be hard for Hon Hai to get the approval from local government to activate more production lines, as doing so will put existing healthy workers at greater risks.

In short, we believe the full operation of any mega factories in China is unlikely to be seen before the end of Feb. We only expect to see a mild and gradual ramp of production capacities across the industry in coming weeks.

# The impact on the demand side

The supply chain disruption isn’t our main concern. In our view, the demand softness could hurt the share prices of tech supply chain more. We believe the uncertainty (of economic outlook) and the anxiety of consumers will lead to soft demand in coming months.

Based on our latest channel checks, we believe Huawei has adjusted 1Q20 smartphone production orders from \~48mn to \~40mn (down a third from 60mn+ in 1Q19). Peers such ash Oppo and Vivo have also trimmed orders by 15%-20%. They all high sales exposure to China.

Samsung smartphone production could see high single digit YoY decline. Apple supply chain is the only bright spot, with iPhone and AirPod production up 25%+ and 40%+ YoY due to hot sales of iPhone 11 and AirPod Pro. But we expect Samsung and Apple likely to cut down orders too should demand adversely impacted by the 2019-nCoV spreading more wildly.

# Investment recommendation – Apple/Samsung supply chain could stay relatively resilient

It is inevitable the smartphone OEMs and the entire supplier chain will be adversely impacted by the 2019-nCoS. That being said, we expect vendors with have higher Apple (Largan, Catcher and Luxshare) and Samsung (SEMCO) exposure to suffer less. From the supply angle, the Samsung supply chain will be less impacted as most of its production sites are based in Vietnam and Korea. From the demand angle, we expect iPhone supply chain to stay relatively resilient, given the hots sales and continued shortage of iPhone 11 and AirPod Pro.

On the longer term, we prefer companies with technology leadership, pricing power and content value growth. They include Largan, Sony, Qualcomm, LG Innotec. Refer to our recent thematic report (dated 12 January 2020) for more detail.
