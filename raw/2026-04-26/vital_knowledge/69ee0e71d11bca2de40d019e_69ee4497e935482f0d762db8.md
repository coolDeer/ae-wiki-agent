Iran

- Iran had been insisting since Friday morning that its foreign minister, despite embarking on a multi-city tour that included a stop in Islamabad (the capital city of the war’s key negotiating intermediatory), did not intend to meet with US officials , and it made good on that promise . After Iran’s foreign minister departed Pakistan Sat morning, Trump called off a planned visit by Witkoff and Kushner to Islamabad .
- The news is modestly disappointing , especially since stocks on Friday rallied (in part) in anticipation of a face-to-face exchange of proposals , but it’s hardly devastating, and (like so much else about this topic lately) really amounts to more noise than anything else .
- Trump on Saturday didn’t sound too bothered by the cancelled trip – he told Axios that the Iranians can “call us if they want… we can do it just as well by telephone ” and when asked if the war was about to resume said “ no, it doesn’t mean that ”. Trump implied that immediately after calling off the Witkoff and Kushner trip , the US received a new proposal from Iran that was “ better than before ”, but still “ not good enough ”. Meanwhile, Iran’s foreign minister indicated that he shared with Pakistan his country’s position on a “workable framework to permanently end the war” , and Pakistan’s PM on Saturday said he held a “construction telephone exchange” with the president of Iran . The US-Iran ceasefire remains in effect (Trump last week extended it indefinitely), and the Israel-Lebanon détente was just pushed out by three weeks on Friday (although the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire is very fragile given that the IDF and Hezbollah are still very much engaged in a war ).
- The divided state of the Iranian government is a major impediment to the negotiating process – various factions, with different agendas and objectives, are vying for influence and preventing Tehran from speaking to the US with a single voice .
- We continue to think Trump has no good options for military escalation , which is why he seems increasingly disinclined to resume the bombing campaign or commit grounds troops (the economic and political fallout appear too great for the White House to bear, Iran still has a significant capacity for attacking critical infrastructure in the region and will inevitability retaliate if struck again, and the Pentagon needs to be mindful of its depleted munition stockpiles). Signaling from the White House over the last several weeks that Operation Epic Fury is very likely past its kinetic peak has been a key driver of the equity rally (along with strong earnings). All that said, the Hormuz maritime landscape (with the US and Iran both imposing blockades) is a tinderbox that could trigger unplanned escalation . In addition, Iran is aware of Trump’s wariness for kinetic escalation , which is why they’re digging in their heels .
- Both sides are stuck . Trump is unwilling to escalate militarily , unable to exit completely , and forced to impose a Hormuz blockade that’s economically untenable for an extended period. But the status quo is hardly sustainable for Iran either .
- The current strategy of imposing an economic chokehold on Iran remains in effect and seems to be broadening as the Pentagon interdicts ships beyond the Middle East and the Treasury imposes additional sanctions (the Treasury on Friday unveiled sanctions against a slew of shipping firms and a Chinese refiner with ties to Iran). Iran’s economy was already drowning before the war started, and is undoubtably feeling acute pain at the moment , while pressure rises on its oil industry (with exports frozen and storage tanks filling up, the country could soon be forced to make some difficult choices).
- The blockade seems to be a major stumbling block for Iran and a key factor (along with the infighting plaguing its government) preventing a second face-to-face meeting , which is why we wouldn’t be surprised to see Trump suspend it for a brief period to help accelerate the negotiating process .
- Bottom Line : we continue to think a US-Iran “deal” is much more likely than not, but: 1) this is largely anticipated already by investors (which means the consummation of an actual agreement probably won’t trigger meaningful further gains); 2) there is still a risk that the situation escalates militarily (the odds of this are low, but stocks will fall much more on a “bombing resumes” headline than they will rally on a “deal is reached” one); 3) the best-case outcome seems like a vague and non-binding JCPOA-like understanding that fails to address all the core, underlying sources of contention; 4) even if the conflict completely ended today, there is going to be a months-long (at least) stagflationary fallout from what’s already taken place (Procter & Gamble on Friday noted that oil staying at current levels would amount to a \$1B incremental cost headwind next fiscal year, and that’s not something they will just absorb – prices will move up, and costs will be slashed further, and they are hardly unique); and 5) Hormuz will never be the same again (it will take months after a deal is struck for supply chains to normalize and mines to be swept out of the water, but even then the market will permanently impose a geopolitical risk premium tax on anything moving through the Strait).
