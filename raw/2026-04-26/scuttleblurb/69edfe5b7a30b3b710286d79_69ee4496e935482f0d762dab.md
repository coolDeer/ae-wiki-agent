For some of these names, one can propose idiosyncratic reasons that might explain the sell-off – Shift4 is an opaque and levered serial acquirer; Global Payments and Fiserv are sclerotic, share-donating incumbents; PayPal took an ambitious strategy and executed it disastrously. Adyen, by contrast, operates a unified, organically developed platform, unencumbered by debt and guided by operators with skin in the game. But its stock, too, has collapsed. Having swooned 65% from the highs, it is not much higher than it was 6 years ago, even as payment volumes have sextupled and EBITDA has quintupled. This can sometimes happen to a stock that starts off at \~125x earnings!

Adyen’s commercial success has been supported by 2 unique pillars that should be familiar to most of us by now.

First, unlike legacy merchant acquirers, who spent decades assembling a patchwork of disparate providers through M&A, Adyen created, entirely in-house, a single unified that extends across all its markets. Whereas First Data and Global Payments, which operate more like an archipelago of payment platforms, must build the same product or feature multiple times to accommodate different code bases, Adyen need only do so once. Second, whereas most peers, including modern PSPs like Stripe, still rely on aggregators and BIN rentals to serve different markets, Adyen holds banking licenses in every country in which it operates, giving it greater visibility into transaction details, which in turn translates to superior authorization rates. As I explained in some thoughts on Adyen:

Combining the gateway with merchant acquiring and processing means Adyen can underwrite merchants according to their own compliance standards and save on fees they would otherwise pay a third party acquirer. But more importantly, it gives them greater visibility into and control over the payments flow.

As an acquiring bank with direct access to card rails, Adyen can iteratively tweak messaging details in an authorization request to improve the chances that the issuer bank will accept it. If a payment fails due to a connection issue at the level of the acquiring bank or processor, they can more easily see and rapidly fix it than most other payment service providers, who obviously can’t inspect the systems of the acquiring banks they rent BINS from. Adyen, more so than any other PSP, is very opinionated about owning the full stack. They would rather not process payments in a country at all than gateway to third party acquirers, whose variable performance dilutes their exacting service standards.

These do not, on the surface, seem like much of a moat. Stripe, First Data, or any other processor could have made the same architectural and strategic choices early in their lives. I suspect one underappreciated reason they didn’t is that the payments industry is saturated with FOMO. There is always a plausible-sounding urgent narrative for why BNPL or crypto or digital wallets or some other new thing needs to be squeezed into your roadmap now. Whether due to a cautious European temperament or the personality quirks of its founders, Adyen stands apart in its restraint. As competitors acquired targets in a frenzied attempt to keep pace with the latest themes, Adyen dedicated itself to strengthening its two foundational pillars, recognizing that a unified tech stack and vertical integration would remain relevant regardless of what new payment fashions emerged.

That discipline has produced a genuinely differentiated product. But Adyen prices for it accordingly, which has invited a familiar critique. Some have argued that in a competitive US market where payments is often treated as a commodity, Adyen’s relatively high processing fees are unsustainable. That concern came to a head in August 2023, when the company swiftly shed \~60% of its market cap after management cited increasing competitive pressure in North America. Net revenue growth there had decelerated sharply from 45% in 2h22 to 23% in 1h23. PayPal was partly to blame, having effectively given away Braintree processing at cost in an effort to drive volumes toward its higher-margin branded checkout button. It didn’t help that EBITDA margins were simultaneously compressed by an aggressive hiring spree that had not yet been absorbed.

There is some truth to the claim that online payments processing has been commoditized. But the cost of processing extends well beyond the explicit fee. When you factor in superior authorization rates and the lower back-office reconciliation burden that come from running a unified platform across all regions and channels, Adyen may well offer the lowest total cost of ownership for the enterprise merchants it serves, the kind that process tens of billions in payment volume and for whom even a few 10s of basis points of incremental approvals translate to meaningful profit dollars.

In any case, PayPal’s loss-leading gambit flopped and Braintree retreated to profitable growth. Adyen’s North American revenue growth reaccelerated over the following year, ending 2h25 up 26% y/y ex. fx, faster than during the Braintree scare and on nearly twice the revenue base. Growth has admittedly slowed from the 45%- 50% pace reported in 2022, but even so Adyen is clearly taking share and, with margins expanding from 46% in 2023 to 53% last year, doing so profitably, which reinforces management’s view that payments is not entirely a commodity and product differentiation matters too.

This is especially true in omnichannel. For large enterprises with global footprints that process payments both online and in-store, Adyen’s Unified Commerce is widely considered the cleanest solution available. Its advantage here is a direct consequence of the same dogmatic commitment to a single platform…because online and in-store transactions run on the same stack, merchants can offer seamless BOPIS experiences to shoppers and mine transaction data across channels without stitching together separate systems. Unified Commerce found initial traction among luxury retailers, merchants especially attuned to the consumer experience, but has since expanded into hospitality, entertainment, and food and beverage. Processed volumes have exploded from €45bn in 2019 to €442bn last year (now 32% of Adyen’s total) and with roughly 85% of global payment volume still occurring in person, the runway ahead is massive.

The same unified global stack undergirding Unified Commerce also sets the groundwork for a third foundational layer: Dynamic ID, which management describes as an intelligence layer embedded within the payments platform. On the merchant side, Dynamic ID simplifies onboarding and KYC. If a newly onboarded merchant is transacting with customers already known to Adyen’s network, or using bank accounts and cards observed elsewhere on the platform, that raises the odds that the business is legitimate, allowing Adyen to streamline or bypass the usual requests for incorporation documents, bank statements, and other supporting paperwork that will, in any case, be increasingly trivial to forge with AI.

On the consumer side, Dynamic ID recognizes shopper behavioral patterns across transactions. Because Adyen processes both online and in-store payments on the same platform, it can see that a particular card was tapped at a coffee shop this morning – proof that a physical person, not a bot, is behind the credential – which raises confidence that an online transaction later that day using the same card is legitimate. Likewise, a hotel booking in Rome is more likely to be legitimate if followed by the purchase of a plane ticket there. And a card used online is more likely to be fraudulent if it hasn’t been swiped at a store for months. Behavioral data can also be used to combat issues like refund abuse, where a customer might claim an item never arrived when it did, or reseller fraud, where shoppers buy limited-edition products or promotional services and resell them on gray markets.

Dynamic ID feeds directly into the total cost of ownership advantage I discussed earlier. Higher authorization rates only benefit a merchant if they reflect genuinely good transactions, not liberal risk parameters that simply permit dubious ones that generate chargebacks and returns down the line. But playing it too safe means turning away legitimate customers. As Matt Levine once put it, “the right amount of fraud is not zero”. Every transaction involves a trade-off between fraud risk and friction, and Adyen, by virtue of its unmatched visibility across both online and in-person channels, is uniquely positioned to optimize it. And the better Adyen gets at this, the more merchants it attracts and the more wallet share it captures, generating still more signal to refine identity and risk models in classic flywheel fashion.

The second Adyen differentiator – its ownership of banking licenses, so central to transaction visibility and the higher authorization rates it enables – also underpins its recent push beyond payments. A section from management’s latest shareholder letter, titled “From payments to global financial infrastructure,” lays out the concept of Intelligent Money Movement, in which Adyen acts as an orchestrator for fund flows. The service is relevant for enterprise merchants but is especially valuable for Adyen’s platform customers, which account for roughly 15% of processing volume. That category was initially limited to marketplaces like eBay and GoFundMe, where Adyen onboarded sellers, split payments between the marketplace and seller, and facilitated payouts across countries and currencies. But through that work, Adyen recognized a broader opportunity serving software platforms that serve small businesses.

Consider a fictitious vertical software platform, RestSoft, that provides independent restaurants with point of sale, online ordering, payroll, and other tools. After Adyen processes a diner’s payment, it carves out RestSoft’s platform fees and deposits the balance into the restaurant’s Adyen-hosted account. If the restaurant receives many small payments throughout the day, RestSoft might sweep a single net amount to the restaurant’s external bank account at Wells Fargo each night or week, after subtracting refunds, disputes, and other adjustments.

RestSoft, through Adyen, might also issue branded business debit cards that the restaurant uses to buy supplies or cover delivery expenses, drawing on funds held in its balance account. And through Adyen Capital, RestSoft could offer a cash advance, which the restaurant could spend through its issued cards or receive as a deposit to its Wells Fargo account.

Adyen’s card issuing volumes, while still miniscule compared to its payment volumes, are becoming just large enough to matter, having grown 8x over the past year to €6bn. The number of payouts (transfers between Adyen and external banks), meanwhile, is growing 40% y/y, reaching €10s of billions per month.

These services are hardly new. Adyen began offering debit card issuance in 2019 and Capital in 2022. Nor was Adyen first to market. Stripe, Square, and PayPal launched their own versions years earlier. The same could be said of Dynamic ID. Surely, Adyen has always used behavioral patterns across channels to pick up on fraud signals. And Uplift, the product suite built around Dynamic ID, packages capabilities – fraud tools, payment optimization, authentication – that the company has offered in some form for years.

The new labels and presentation are, in part, repackaging and rebranding. Adyen is trying to broaden the perceived scope of what it does. With payments processing becoming somewhat commoditized at the same time that Issuing and Capital are transitioning from experimental adjacency to commercial relevancy, Intelligent Money Movement signals to the market that Adyen is embracing a new, more competitively insulated role as a financial operating system that spans payments, balances, payouts, cards, and embedded finance.

In leveraging cross-merchant data and behavioral signals to detect fraud and filter out illegitimate transactions, Dynamic ID is, in one sense, just a new label for a longstanding capability. What’s changed is that management now positions this intelligence as a core identity layer beneath a wider range of products across the platform. Uplift, for instance, consists of modules addressing different stages of the payment funnel – authentication, authorization, routing. Dynamic ID makes each of them better. A confident read on shopper identity and intent allows Adyen to improve authorization rates, block fewer legitimate transactions, strip out unnecessary authentication friction, and steer shoppers toward lower-cost payment methods only when doing so is unlikely to hurt conversion. At Adyen’s 2025 Investor Day, an executive at JustEat reported that adopting Uplift increased conversions by 1% and cut false positives – legitimate transactions wrongly flagged and blocked – in half.

What you’ll notice is that every service Adyen offers traces back to its twin pillars. A unified global tech stack aggregates transaction signals across geographies and channels, generating the intelligence behind Dynamic ID, which in turn powers Uplift. Banking licenses allow Adyen to onboard users, manage merchant funds, issue cards, and layer on financial products with greater reliability and speed than a provider dependent on third parties. Together, these capabilities lift sales and reduce the total cost of payments for merchants, drawing in more volume, which further sharpens the system’s intelligence.

Does AI add friction to this well-oiled flywheel or even dismantle it completely? Maybe SaaSpocalypse is spilling over into payments. After all, software has been one of the industry’s most important distribution channels for payments. Adyen thinks \~2/3s of SMBs will eventually gain access to payments through SaaS. Around 15% of its processing volumes last year came through integrations with third party platforms. But even if AI rendered large swaths of third party software extinct (a big if, in my opinion!), it doesn’t follow that the embedded payments functionality goes away. Dentists, salon owners, contractors, and coffee shops still need to be paid somehow.

One might argue that frontier model developers will eventually own the payments layer. So they’re going to run KYC checks, verify merchants, hold funds, interface with networks and banks, manage chargebacks and disputes, hire a direct sales force to pitch enterprises, and bear legal liability as a regulated financial institution? I don’t think this is a good use of time and resources for a frontier lab spending hundreds of billions training increasingly powerful models. Notably, OpenAI recently paused its Instant Checkout initiative, recognizing that the checkout flow is better handled by apps that plug into ChatGPT than embedded directly in its results:

The best consumer experiences are powered by deep partnership with merchants. To build these deep partnerships, we want to offer merchants options for how they convert consumers. We’ve found that the initial version of Instant Checkout did not offer the level of flexibility that we aspire to provide, so we’re allowing merchants to use their own checkout experiences while we focus our efforts on product discovery.

Does agentic commerce present disruptive potential? It’s still early enough that everyone is squinting in the dark, trying to discern its eventual shape. Adyen is working with OpenAI, Visa, Mastercard, and Google to develop protocols, but management is quick to emphasize that things remain experimental, with meaningful volumes from agentic transactions still years away. Other processors say the same.

But assume AI moves beyond the current discovery phase – where shoppers use ChatGPT to research and compare products – and that agents eventually become ubiquitous in selecting and purchasing them. Even in that scenario, humans are not excised from the loop. They will still need to specify some key parameters to the agent: “buy groceries this week up to \$150,” “book a flight under \$800 on these airlines,” “renew this subscription only if the price has not increased,” “find me pair of sneakers size 11.5 for under \$300 and have it delivered by Christmas” etc. Basically, the user is telling the agent to spend on their behalf according to specified rules…which sounds awfully similar to the programmable payment credentials that are already widely in use today.

To book reservations, online travel agencies routinely generate virtual cards that are valid only for a specific stay at a specific hotel for a specific amount. Corporate card programs often come with daily or monthly spending caps and place restrictions on the types of products and services that can be purchased. Certain fleet cards, for example, only work at gas stations but not at grocery stores or coffee shops.

You can think of agentic commerce as an extension of delegated payment authority, leveraging tokenization technology that is already widely in use. At the network level, Visa and Mastercard would issue tokens to AI agents that confirm the agent is acting on behalf of a specific authenticated user and carrying valid instructions to make a purchase. A PSP like Adyen would then apply an added measure of security and policy enforcement at the merchant level, using behavioral signals and transaction patterns to decide how much scrutiny to apply to an actual transaction. Even a “real” agent can make unintended purchases based on ambiguous instructions.

Like, maybe you tell an agent to buy a computer and it orders 10 laptops for overnight delivery to an old address or something. Or maybe the agent is exploiting return policies by systematically purchasing items, requesting refunds, and keeping the merchandise or buying up limited-inventory products in bulk to flip on secondary markets, violating the merchant’s terms of sale. Just because you have a valid visa to enter a country doesn’t mean you can do whatever the hell you want once you’re there.

Also, risk decisioning need not only play a restrictive role in commerce. Adyen’s integrated presence across both digital and in-person transactions allows it to validate otherwise suspicious-looking agent activity that a digital-only processor might reject. An agent’s 3am online purchase looks less like fraud if it can be linked to a real person who bought something from that same merchant in-store earlier that day.

Meanwhile, although the front-end consumer experience is likely transformed in an agentic world, I don’t think backend payments processes fundamentally change. The traditional payments flow – checkout → gateway → merchant acquirer → card network → issuer → approval – remains the same, as do the challenges of onboarding merchants, authenticating shoppers, detecting fraud, optimizing auth rates, and routing payments.

It’s just that Adyen’s value prop may migrate increasingly toward trust functions, like verifying agent identities and transactions.

In short, an agent-maxxed world introduces more complexity, further reinforcing the role of payment providers who can abstract it away for merchants. In much the same way Adyen currently allows merchants manage multiple payment methods and sales channels, it will also help promote legitimate agent shopping activity and curb suspicious transactions. Moreover, the widespread adoption of agents should expand the volume of transactions, both by making it easier to find relevant products and preventing leakage at checkout, where many consumers bail.

Stablecoins are the other big “current thing” in payments. They are very far from mainstream adoption at the point-of-sale in the developed markets where Adyen primarily operates1, so it’s not a priority for the company today. But were this to change for some reason – see my Wise and Remitly write-ups for why that is far from inevitable – Adyen would simply accept them as it would any other payment method.

Having gained some comfort that the sweeping technology themes of the day don’t pose existential risks to Adyen, we’re left with a competitively advantaged share gainer trading at its lowest valuation since its 2018 IPO. That’s not to say the sell-off over the past year was unwarranted. Three years ago, the stock was priced at \~70x earnings, with investors anticipating mid-20s to low-30s net revenue growth over the medium term. In late 2023, management revised that guidance to a low/high-20s, then delivered at the low end through 2025 as volume growth among existing customers – particularly a handful of large online retailers in Asia and luxury merchants – decelerated. This year, management is again guiding to low-20s growth, but unlike the prior two years, increased investment in headcount means that growth comes with no margin expansion.

Whether the deceleration reflects passing market headwinds or structural drag from Adyen’s growing scale, the company should, for all the reasons discussed, continue gaining wallet share, which at hsd/ldd makes up the single largest component of growth.

Revenue growth has downshifted from 30%-40% to the low 20s, but in re-setting to a low-20s multiple on this year’s earnings the stock, in my opinion, has over-corrected to the downside. For a consistent share gainer likely to compound earnings by \~20% over the foreseeable future, with little evidence of franchise impairment, the sell off feels too punitive.
