# Day 1 — February 26, 2026

Harvey — Semiconductor Analyst, Taipei

The semiconductor landscape in 2026 looks nothing like 2025.

Last year, you could get by just reading the weekly direction of the AI narrative. Figure out whether the market was favoring AI acceleration and hardware, or AI stagnation and software, and you had your playbook. NVDA, AVGO, TSM were the AI Narrative. You didn’t need to understand how LLMs were reshaping memory demand, how scale-up versus scale-out was transforming optics, what semicap expansion timelines looked like, or which labs TPU had penetrated.

That era is over.

NVDA and AVGO, because of their sheer value share and growing margin concerns, no longer track short-term swings in AI sentiment the way they used to. Frontier model development has moved past pure training bottlenecks into system-level capability constraints. What determines who wins is no longer peak single-chip performance. It is the system layer: interconnect, topology, scheduling, cooling, power, and full software stack coordination.

Long-horizon Agents have begun shipping in production. AI’s effective working radius has expanded from single-turn inference to long-chain engineering tasks and business workflows. The binding constraint is no longer per-inference FLOPS. It is context management and sustained execution. The memory supply chain has changed completely as a result.

When GPU performance scales 30x per generation while optical bandwidth scales only 1x, the entire value distribution of the industry starts shifting violently.

Being a good semi analyst keeps getting harder. Frontier model breakthroughs move on a monthly cadence. The supply chain produces a new technical catalyst every few weeks. Finding opportunities is no longer limited to the familiar large caps. We have had to push deeper into mid caps and small caps, and we have started to abandon the cyclical frameworks we grew up on.

In just its first year, FundaAI captured opportunities in LITE, COHR, TSEM, SNDK, MU, AMAT, INTC, and others. We need to capture even more.

So I pointed Funda at my universe and asked a simple question: Hi Funda, when you detect that a semiconductor stock shows strong sentiment or a significant sentiment change, please look across its supply chain (upstream, downstream, and peers) to identify stocks whose sentiment may change rapidly or that could benefit from the move. Notify me when there are notable changes.

Funda came back with LITE showing unmistakable sentiment shift. Optics was leading everything else by a wide margin.

I saved it as a Regular Play — a persistent, auto-refreshing dashboard I could check every morning before Asia opened.

That Regular Play came in handy fast. AAOI was reporting after the close.

I first ran an AAOI Review through Funda.

Then, in the Semi Sentiment Play, I saw it.

# Day 2 — February 27, 2026

Marsh — Research Operations & Software/Macro Narrative, Hong Kong

I spent the entire day refreshing Twitter and Polymarket.

Iran was heating up. Polymarket was pricing a low probability of open conflict within the next week. So why were crude and gold both moving in the premarket?

What happened last March was still fresh. I did not want another ride like that. If this March was going to rhyme with last March, we needed hedge ideas before the tape told us.

Polymarket is a consensus tool. It tells you what the crowd thinks. What I needed was a leading indicator tool.

Something that watched what people do, not what they say.

So I asked Funda to run our monitoring agents: Global Air Traffic Movement, Global Maritime Vessel Movement, Shipping Insurance Rates, Crude Oil Export Volume. Within minutes, it had compiled a set of leading indicators and assessed the probability of Strait of Hormuz disruption.

> Only a Strait closure would transmit a regional conflict into a global macro event. That was the risk I needed to price.

Funda mapped the impact analysis across all risk assets and generated trigger thresholds. When those thresholds were hit, it would push me an alert.

I saved the entire monitoring package as a Regular Play with a live tactical satellite map of the Persian Gulf. Now I had everything in one place: live risk probabilities, cross-asset impact analysis, and real-time sentiment. All updating continuously.

Most analysts were still debating whether to hedge. I already knew my trigger levels.

I thought today’s story would be all about Iran. My biggest fear was that after a full weekend of headlines fermenting, every semi stock would gap down hard on Monday.

Then NVDA announced investments in LITE and COHR.

That headline rescued the entire optics sector.

My gut told me that during a period of active geopolitical risk, a sector rallying this hard against the tape usually signals sentiment is peaking. Especially after AAOI had run 57% in a single session the prior Friday.

I pulled up my Semi Sentiment Play.

Optics sentiment was leading every other semi sector by a wide margin. LITE was number one.

On the very first day that Macro Narrative turned ugly, LITE’s sentiment was printing at an extreme. I knew there had to be risk building. I just didn’t know what would trigger it.

In this business, the hardest thing isn’t finding the trade. It’s knowing when the risk is building.

# Day 4 — March 3, 2026

Harvey — Semiconductor Analyst, Taipei

Chen — Space Analyst, Dublin

I was still thinking about yesterday’s question. What would trigger the optics sentiment reversal? Based on past patterns, something was coming.

Because I’m based in Taiwan, my daytime hours overlap with the Taiwan, China, Japan, and Korean sessions. I watch all of them. Today the outlier was Korea.

> By midday, the KOSPI was already down 5%. A market that had rallied nearly every session for the past two months.

Could Korea be the X-factor? I pulled in Chen, our space analyst, and we ran the question through Funda. The root cause came back as three reinforcing drivers.

Korea’s index was a leveraged semiconductor bet by construction. Samsung and SK Hynix alone made up roughly 45% of KOSPI weight. When AI and semiconductor sentiment reverses, KOSPI moves two to three times the regional average. The Iran shock hit at the same moment a domestic FSS margin lending probe spooked retail investors who had been aggressively buying dips on margin.

Then came the piece that connected everything.

I asked Funda which U.S. stocks were most heavily held by Korean retail investors. The answer made it obvious.

Yesterday LITE was number one in US semi sentiment. Today I noticed that LITE was also the most heavily added single US equity position among Korean retail investors.

“The biggest loser tonight is probably going to be LITE, isn’t it?” I muttered to myself. Even though it was my favorite name.

The information was there. It was always there. It just took three separate analytical layers — sentiment tracking, macro catalyst identification, and cross-border flow analysis — converging at once. No human could hold all of that in working memory simultaneously. But a system that had already been trained on our analytical instincts could.

# Day 5 — March 4, 2026

Wooding — Software Analyst, Singapore

Software investing this year has been brutal. But that’s not what I want to talk about.

I want to talk about why every “AI-powered research tool” I’ve tried in the last twelve months has disappointed me. And I’ve tried a lot of them.

They all do the same thing. You type a question, you get a summary. A reasonably coherent paragraph, occasionally with a chart. It reads like a smart intern who skimmed the 10-K overnight.

This is the dirty secret of the AI-for-finance boom: the output only looks impressive. It doesn’t warn you about the LITE reversal or the Korean margin liquidation or the Hormuz trigger levels. It summarizes. It does not think.

Here’s why. The architecture is wrong.

Every AI research tool I’ve seen starts from the same premise: take a large language model, plug it into a financial data feed, and let the user ask questions. This is prompt engineering dressed up as product. It has no instinct for what matters in your coverage universe. It cannot build on its own prior analysis. Every session starts from zero. And behind the scenes, it's no better: they have no proprietary data, and no ability to recruit professional analysts to help label and curate what they feed into the model.

> An analyst who resets to zero every morning is not an analyst. It is a search engine with better grammar.

The reason those tools feel flat is the same reason a tourist’s observations about a city feel flat. They can describe what they see. They cannot tell you what changed since last week, what’s about to change next week, or why the change matters for the three other things you’re watching. That requires accumulated context. It requires memory.

At FundaAI, we solve this differently. When I build a Skill — say, tracking frontier lab hiring patterns and mapping them to software verticals at risk — that Skill doesn’t disappear when I close the window. It persists. It compounds. It feeds into a Play that refreshes daily and pushes me an alert when something meaningful shifts.

The Play remembers what the baseline was. It knows what “normal” looks like for Anthropic’s hiring page versus two weeks ago. It can tell me that OpenAI just tripled its healthcare domain expert postings without me having to ask.

Back in December, we published deep dives on how the frontier labs were partnering with consulting firms to deliver enterprise software solutions. Through our interviews, I found that many client requests were bottom-up and modular — the kind of thing AI could now fulfill quickly. The next generation of AI software is no longer one-size-fits-all. It is about understanding the last-mile complexity of each customer’s workflow, then solving it in an atomic way.

Isn’t that exactly the same problem in research? Every analyst’s workflow is different. Every coverage universe has its own rhythms, its own signals, its own cross-references that matter. A generic tool cannot capture that.

What captures it is a system that learns from the analyst, not one that lectures to them.

So I built a Play. Funda now monitors what types of industry annotators and domain experts are hiring, maps those hiring signals to software verticals, and maps those verticals to specific publicly traded companies at risk. Three layers, auto-refreshing, pushing me alerts only when something actually moves.

When Funda OS starts seeing healthcare domain expert roles pop up in the wild, it knows a healthcare SaaS name is about to have a very bad quarter. It sees the bullet before it's fired.

The difference between a tool and a weapon is intent. The difference between a chatbot and an analyst is memory.

# Day 6 — March 5, 2026

Andy — Semiconductor Analyst, Singapore

After several days of heavy selling, semi names may have finally found a floor. This looked like a strong entry point.

The single biggest risk in the memory sector is capacity expansion. We run supply chain checks every week and have a strong handle on capacity trends. But when you’re about to size up a position in a sector that just sold off 15%, you want more than a handle. You want convergence.

Convergence is when multiple independent analytical frameworks — each with different assumptions, different data sources, different failure modes — arrive at the same conclusion. One framework saying “buy” is a thesis.

Three frameworks saying “buy” is a position.

I asked Funda to model the full decision timeline: when would SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron each face their capacity expansion trigger points, and in which quarter would the supply-demand inflection appear after ramp.

The model ran against our proprietary supply chain data, cross-referenced with frontier model technology trajectories and production estimates.

The output lined up with what our channel checks were saying. It lined up with what the technology trends implied. Three lenses, same answer.

> Conviction isn’t about being bold. It’s about having done the work three different ways and getting the same answer every time.

This is where most AI tools fall apart. They can run one model. They cannot tell you whether the model’s output is consistent with two other analytical approaches you’ve been building for months. They lack the accumulated context to cross-validate. They give you a number. They don’t give you confidence.

Funda gave me confidence, because Funda had been accumulating our supply chain intelligence for months. It didn’t start from a blank page. It started from everything we already knew.

An AI that starts from zero gives you a model. An AI that starts from everything you’ve already learned gives you an edge.

# Day 7 — March 6, 2026

I’ve been in ongoing conversations with our head of product, Yujia, about what the future of a Research Agent should actually look like. After this week, the answer feels clearer than ever.

When coding agents took a massive leap forward at the end of December, I knew we were looking at the future. But the future of what?

Look at the landscape. There are now a dozen of companies selling “AI research assistants” to the buy side.

They treat “research” as a question-answering problem. But research is not question-answering. Research is pattern recognition across time. It is the slow accumulation of context that allows you to recognize, in real time, that Korean retail margin liquidation will transmit through to US optics names tonight. No amount of RAG and vector search will give you that. The insight requires having been in the seat, watching the flows, building the mental model, for months.

> The AI research tools are trying to replace the analyst. The right approach is to extend the analyst. To take everything an experienced investor has already learned and make it run at machine speed.

That is what we mean by Skills and Plays. A Skill is a codified analytical instinct. Harvey's understanding that Korean retail investor behavior can transmit to specific US equities and create reversal signals — that's a Skill. Marsh's framework for monitoring Strait of Hormuz risk through leading indicators rather than prediction markets — that's a Skill. Wooding's method of tracking frontier lab hiring as a leading indicator for software disruption — that's a Skill.

A Play combines Skills into a persistent, auto-refreshing analytical pipeline. It compounds. It remembers. It pushes you an alert not when something happened, but when something is about to change relative to what you already know. That distinction is everything.

The speed at which vertical experience translates into product is the only metric that matters. A typical AI agent company takes two weeks from product research to PM spec to engineering deployment. At FundaAI, an analyst builds a Skill in the morning, a PM validates stability that afternoon, and engineers ship it by end of day. The reason we can do this is that our analysts are building the product, not describing it to someone else to build.

To make this system even more differentiated, we are building our own data infrastructure. The most valuable database in finance is an expert interview repository — structured, searchable, cross-referenced, and annotated by the same analysts who use it every day. We are accumulating those interviews at a pace of hundreds per week, all produced by our in-house team. That is a compounding advantage. Every new interview makes every future Skill and Play slightly sharper. Every new Skill makes the next interview slightly more targeted.

The agent companies of the future will be operationally intensive companies.

Do not compete head-on with the frontier labs. Everyone has access to the same foundation models. Data format alignment, fiscal-versus-calendar year tricks, precision hacks — those had value three months ago. Today they are trivially reproducible. The durable moat comes from the ability to directly and rapidly convert experience and know-how into agent capabilities. This looks a lot more like the operationally intensive internet companies of the last era than it does a typical AI startup.

There are thousands upon thousands of unique analytical instincts across the equity industry. They are sitting in the heads of portfolio managers, sector analysts, macro strategists. They should not be reduced to formulas.

They should be turned into living systems that compound over time.
