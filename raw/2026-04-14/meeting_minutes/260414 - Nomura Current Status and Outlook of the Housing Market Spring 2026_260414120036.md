**Q: What is the impact of the Financial Services Agency's recent warning to regional banks on real estate lending?**
A: The warning is not a "Reiwa-era total volume regulation" and is not intended to cut off a major source of funding. Its primary purpose is to enforce stricter and more rigorous adherence to existing rules to prevent overly cozy relationships between banks and developers. However, it has already had the following effects:
1.  **Increased Difficulty for Developers:** Some small and medium-sized developers are already finding it more difficult to procure development funds.
2.  **Continued Viability:** Cross-border lending remains a viable option for regional banks as long as the rules are strictly followed. The situation should be assessed on a case-by-case basis.

**Q: What are the significant revisions to the mortgage tax deduction system starting from the 2026 fiscal year, and what is the policy's primary goal?**
A: The primary goal is to strongly promote the purchase of existing (used) homes by elevating their incentives to be on par with, or even better than, new homes. Key revisions include:
1.  **Relaxed Area Requirement:** The minimum eligible area for both new and used homes is reduced from 50 to 40 square meters (registered internal area).
2.  **Stricter Rules for New Homes:**
    *   **Lower Loan Limits:** For homes meeting the basic energy-efficiency standard, the loan limit is lowered to 30 million JPY for child-rearing/young-adult households and 20 million JPY for general households.
    *   **Future Ineligibility:** From April 2028, new homes that only meet this basic energy-efficiency standard will no longer be eligible for the mortgage deduction.
    *   **Disaster Zone Exclusion:** From 2028, new homes built in disaster "red zones" will be ineligible.
3.  **Enhanced Incentives for Used Homes:**
    *   **Preferential Treatment:** Introduces a distinction between child-rearing/young-adult households and general households, applying the same 30 million/20 million JPY loan limits as new homes.
    *   **Extended Deduction Period:** For energy-efficient used homes, the deduction period is extended from 10 to 13 years, matching new homes.
    *   **Post-2028 Advantage:** After April 2028, a used home meeting only the basic energy-efficiency standard can still receive a deduction (on a 20 million JPY loan over 10 years), whereas a new home of the same standard cannot.
4.  **Special Condition:** To receive the bonus loan limit incentive, child-rearing/young-adult households must purchase a home of 50 square meters or more.

**Q: Besides the mortgage deduction, what other major housing-related tax changes are being implemented?**
A: Other significant tax changes include:
1.  **Inheritance Tax on Rental Properties:** Starting January 2027, the valuation method will be revised. Properties acquired or built within 5 years of an inheritance will be valued at 80% of their market price, which is expected to increase inheritance taxes. This does not apply to properties built on land held for over five years.
2.  **Consumption Tax on Brokerage Fees for Foreigners:** Brokerage fees for non-resident foreigners buying and selling domestic real estate, previously treated as a tax-exempt export, will become taxable at the 10% consumption tax rate.
3.  **Capital Gains Tax on Short-Term Condo Sales:** While not finalized, a likely measure is to significantly increase the capital gains tax rate for very short-term sales (e.g., within 1-2 years) to a level higher than the current 40%, possibly to 60% or 80%, to curb speculation.

**Q: What is the government's strategy regarding housing subsidies and performance standards, and how does Japan's insulation performance compare internationally?**
A: The government's strategy is to incentivize upgrading the existing housing stock. Key points are:
1.  **Subsidies:** A substantial budget of 380 billion JPY is allocated for home renovations (e.g., insulation upgrades) in 2026. This is down from 448 billion JPY in the previous year but remains significant.
2.  **Policy Direction:** The government encourages a "buy-renovate" model: purchase a used home, benefit from the mortgage tax deduction, and use subsidies to improve its insulation and performance.
3.  **International Comparison:** Japan's current housing energy-efficiency and insulation standards are quite low compared to current global standards. The new standards being phased in (ZEH by 2030, GX-ZEH by 2027) are designed to progressively raise performance to be closer to international parity.
4.  **Rating System Overhaul:** In 2030, the government plans to re-base the performance rating system, effectively making the current standard (grade 4) the new bottom of the scale to facilitate future upgrades.

**Q: How have prices for used condominiums and detached houses trended in major metropolitan areas, and what causes the difference?**
A: There is a stark difference in price trends, driven primarily by speculative demand.
1.  **Used Condominiums:**
    *   **Tokyo:** Prices have surged dramatically. In central Tokyo's six wards, the average price increased from 70 million JPY in 2020 to 180 million JPY. This price surge is now beginning to ripple out from central Tokyo to surrounding prefectures.
    *   **Other Cities:** The sharp increase is largely a Tokyo-only phenomenon. Price rises in Osaka and Nagoya have been far more gradual and modest.

3.  **Reason for Disparity:** The price difference is attributed to the weight of speculative demand. Used condos, especially tower mansions in central Tokyo, are treated as assets for investment and tax planning, attracting significant speculative demand. In contrast, detached houses are driven almost exclusively by actual-use demand.

**Q: What are the key factors expected to drive housing prices in the future?**
A: Several cost-push factors and market trends are putting upward pressure on prices:
1.  **Rising Construction Costs:** Labor wages have risen 20-30% since 2021, and building material prices have soared (e.g., aluminum +125%, wire +141%), with no signs of stopping.

    *   **Rental Market:** The massive inflow of singles into central Tokyo is creating a shortage of rental housing for singles (studios, 1K apartments), driving up rental prices.
