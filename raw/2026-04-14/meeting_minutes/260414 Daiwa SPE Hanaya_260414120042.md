## Company Guidance Policy and Market Perception
- **Rationale for Semi-Annual vs. Annual Guidance**
  - The company's shift to semi-annual guidance is seen as a move to align with global standards (e.g., U.S. companies typically don't issue full-year plans).
  - It's a risk-averse strategy to avoid misleading the market, stemming from a past incident where a significant downward revision drew heavy criticism.
  - The core issue is the lack of visibility beyond six months, and the company prefers not to issue guidance for periods it cannot accurately forecast.
- **Potential Market Interpretation and Risks**
  - Issuing only a half-year forecast, especially during a strong market, could be negatively perceived by investors.
  - The market might interpret the move as an indication of a new, unforeseen event that has reduced the company's future visibility.

**Q: Why might a company issue semi-annual guidance instead of a full-year forecast, and what is the market perception of this?**
A: The rationale and market perception are as follows:
1.  **Reason for Semi-Annual Guidance:**
    *   **Alignment with Global Standards:** The company may wish to align with practices of U.S. companies, which typically do not issue annual plans.
    *   **Avoiding Market Misinformation:** The company aims to avoid issuing guidance for periods where visibility is low (beyond six months), as this could mislead the market. This is especially relevant after a previous significant downward revision drew criticism.
    *   **Not Necessarily a Sign of Weakness:** It is not always because the current situation lacks visibility, but a more principled stance on providing reliable forecasts.
2.  **Market Perception Risk:**
    *   If a company only releases a half-year forecast, especially when the market and outlook are strong, it could be negatively perceived.
    *   Investors might interpret it as an indication that an event has occurred which reduces visibility, creating concern.
