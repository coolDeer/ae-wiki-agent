2.  **Mass Production Capacity:** Our significant mass production capacity, primarily established in China over the last 30 years, provides a competitive edge. This was a strategic decision by our founder, Mr. Akira Yamamura, to offer technology at a "China price."
3.  **Production Base Diversification:** In response to US-China trade friction that intensified around 2018-2022, we have diversified our production bases to Malaysia and Japan to mitigate the risks of being solely reliant on China. These new bases are now beginning to contribute to our capacity.
4.  **Global Customer Base:** We have strong relationships with both world-renowned clients (Lam Research, Applied Materials, Tokyo Electron, Kokusai Electric, etc.) and major Chinese SPE companies. Our production presence in China, which has become a major SPE market, allows us to maintain close ties with local customers while serving our established global clients.

**Q: What is your growth potential, mid-term financial plan, and market outlook?**
A: Our growth outlook and plans are as follows:
1.  **Growth Trajectory:** We have experienced significant growth since 2018. Based on customer guidance (e.g., from Lam Research and Applied Materials), we are preparing for another wave of double-digit growth expected in 2025.
2.  **Mid-Term Plan:** We are targeting strong growth with sales goals of 340 billion yen for the next fiscal year and 400 billion yen the year after. We believe our current production capacity is nearly sufficient to support sales of around 400 billion yen.
3.  **Profitability Improvement:** While fixed costs are currently high, we plan to improve profitability by increasing the utilization rate of our factories.
4.  **Market Correlation and Outlook:** Our SPE business has a strong correlation with Wafer Fab Equipment (WFE) investment trends. The maintenance capex run rate is estimated to be in the range of 10 to 15 billion yen. The projected investment for the fiscal year ending March 2027 includes about 10 billion yen for a new factory in Beijing to secure a key Chinese SPE client.

**Q: Regarding the existing convertible bond (CB), are there plans to call it, and would you consider using the CB market for future financing?**

1.  **Soft Call:** We will not comment on whether the soft call option will be exercised, as nothing is decided. However, our original intention was for the bonds to be converted into shares, and we hope holders choose to do so.

Our cash generation has improved significantly, and we have established a three-year share buyback program to offset dilution from the current CB. We would only consider another CB for a major event, such as a large M&A deal that our current cash flow cannot cover.

1.  **Destruction of Production Capacity:** If our production facilities were completely destroyed beyond repair.
2.  **Major Geopolitical Escalation:** A significant expansion of geopolitical conflicts that severely impacts our material supply.
3.  **Natural Disasters:** A major earthquake in Japan could be a factor, but this is unlikely to be a major threat as over 70% of our production capacity is in China, where the risk of major earthquakes is low. The current Middle East situation has minimal direct impact as we source almost no materials from that region, with risks limited to general issues like shipping delays or rising oil prices.

**Q: Are customers willing to pay a pricing premium for non-Chinese manufacturing?**
A: The answer is both yes and no.
1.  **No Premium for "China Plus One":** American manufacturers like Lam Research and Applied Materials are moving sourcing to Malaysia as part of their "China plus one" strategy. For them, it is a necessity, not a choice for which they would pay a premium. Therefore, the price for products from Malaysia is not significantly different from the price in China.
2.  **Winning Trust and New Business:** By investing heavily in Malaysia when our competitors did not, we have gained significant trust from these US clients. This has positioned us to capture more business, including new product lines and a wider scope of components. Our Malaysian factory has become a key hub for collaboration with these clients, which has been highly beneficial. The 25 billion yen raised from our CB was primarily used for this investment.

Our largest customer, Lam Research, is very strong in etching equipment, which has a strong correlation with the memory market. Consequently, we are benefiting from the recent surge in memory prices and capital expenditure, and we expect major orders from them this fiscal year.

**Q: To meet your medium-term plan goals (400 billion yen sales, 11.8% operating margin by March 2028), which areas will drive the largest revenue and margin improvements?**
A: The primary drivers for incremental growth and margin improvement are:
1.  **Ceramics:** This is our highest-margin business, at over 20%. With its production capacity already well-utilized, it will be the main margin driver.
2.  **Quartz:** We expect significant margin improvement for quartz, which is currently just below 10%. As we increase volume and utilization of the upfront investments made in machinery and skilled workers, profitability will rise.
3.  **Parts Cleaning:** The margin for our parts cleaning business is also improving to slightly above our company average, around 12% or 13%. This is driven by Chinese clients moving to single-digit nanometer production, which requires more complex and difficult cleaning processes, allowing us to charge higher prices for our services.

**Q: How do production costs in Malaysia compare with China?**

This is because the Malaysian facility is focused on newer products and contract development projects that are still in the prototyping phase and do not yet have repeat orders. As a result, variable costs are higher compared to our highly efficient Chinese factories that handle repeat orders. We view this as a necessary "seeding process" to widen our business scope and secure future growth, even if it means accepting a slightly lower margin on these future-oriented activities for now.
