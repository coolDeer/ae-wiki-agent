#### State Council issues new rules to counter foreign extraterritorial jurisdiction

**Beijing is strengthening its legal toolkit to push back against what it sees as the overreach of Western sanctions.**

On April 13, the State Council released the Regulations on Countering Improper Foreign Extraterritorial Jurisdiction (Gov.cn).

**In its official interpretation, the Ministry of Justice (MoJ) framed the regs as a necessary response to** (MoJ 1):
- *“Western hegemonism and power politics”*

**Some context:**  China has been a frequent target of US measures that apply American laws beyond its own borders.
- Case in point – Washington’s restrictions on non-US firms supplying Chinese chipmakers like Huawei if their products contain US-origin technology.

**The regulations empower the MoJ to designate foreign measures as “improper” extraterritorial jurisdiction**  (Gov.cn).
- Once designated, all entities and individuals are prohibited from complying unless explicitly exempted.
- Authorities can impose a wide range of countermeasures against the foreign country, across immigration, trade, investment, international cooperation, and foreign aid.
- A new Malicious Entity List targets foreign actors involved in formulating and enforcing such measures with penalties including visa bans, asset freezes, and trade restrictions.

**Get smart:**  The new regulations signal a more proactive stance compared to the Ministry of Commerce’s  2021 blocking rules , which were largely defensive and not once enforced.

**Get smarter:**  The rules sharpen the dual-compliance dilemma for foreign companies operating in China.
- Comply with US sanctions and risk Chinese penalties; comply with Chinese rules and risk US penalties.

**foreign affairs**

#### China discusses Iran with regional leaders

Chinese leaders have got Iran on the brain.

**First up, top diplomat Wang Yi held a call with Pakistani Foreign Minister Mohammad Ishaq Dar and met with the UAE special envoy Khaldoon Khalifa Al Mubarak on April 13.**
- Wang thanked Dar for Pakistan’s role in mediating recent talks between the US and Iran, describing the crisis as being at “a critical turning point” (MoFA 1).
- Wang said to Mubarak that “blocking the Strait of Hormuz is not in the common interest of the international community” and that China was willing to “push for the early return of peace” (MoFA 2).

**On the same day, Premier Li Qiang met with Abu Dhabi’s Crown Prince, Sheikh Khalid bin Mohamed Al Nahyan in Beijing**  (People.cn).
- Li further highlighted China’s communication with regional countries during the crisis and its commitment to ending the war.

**Finally, on April 14, Xi Jinping also met with Sheikh Khalid and put forward four propositions for Middle East peace, namely:**
- Upholding the principle of peaceful coexistence
- Respecting national sovereignty
- Upholding international rule of law
- Balancing development and security

**Hardly groundbreaking, but it’s the thought that counts.**

**Get smart** : The longer the conflict drags on, the more it chips away at Beijing’s sizeable energy reserves, squeezes corporate profits, and snarls the global economy that China’s export machine depends on.

**Our question:**  Will the growing threat to China’s economic interests prompt Beijing to get more assertive with its heretofore low-profile diplomatic efforts?

#### Xi Jinping meets with Spanish Prime Minister in Beijing

Xi Jinping thinks we should all just get along.

**After meeting with Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sanchez in Beijing on April 14, Xi said** (Politico):
- *“Let us establish a stronger bond between China and the European Union.”*
- *“If these powers understand each other and cooperate, it will benefit their societies and also contribute to the stability, peace, and prosperity of the world in this delicate international climate.”*

**Xi said that China and the EU could provide stability in a turbulent world (** Xinhua):
- *“Xi Jinping stressed that the world today is beset by turmoil and faces a contest between justice and power politics.”*
- *“China and Spain are both countries with principles and a strong sense of justice.”*

**Sanchez, who has actively   courted   closer  ties with China, said he is down:**
- *“Spain…is willing to maintain close communication and coordination with China to jointly address challenges including international geopolitics, trade protectionism, and climate change, and to uphold international law and multilateralism.”*

**But Sanchez also hinted that China could be more proactive in solving the Iran crisis** (FT):
- *“I find it very difficult to imagine any parties other than China that could resolve the situation in Iran and the Strait of Hormuz.”*

**Get smart:** Despite Sanchez’s pleas, Beijing sees little upside in wading deeper into the Iran crisis.

**Get smart:** No matter what China does on Iran, an increasingly erratic (and belligerent) United States is making China a more attractive partner – not just for the EU but for countries around the world.

**business environment**

#### Party rolls out reform plan for industry associations and chambers of commerce

**Domestic industry and business groups are getting whipped into shape.**

On April 13, the Party and the State Council jointly released guidelines for reforming industry associations and chambers of commerce.

**We saw this coming:** In March, the State Council paved the way for such reforms by  amending its regulations  governing social organizations.

**The direction is clear:**  Fewer, larger, more tightly supervised, and more politically aligned organizations. As part of the overhaul (Gov.cn):
- Party cells will gain greater influence over leadership appointments and key decisions
- Oversight will tighten, particularly when it comes to foreign engagement and ideological matters
- Associations will be explicitly restricted from forming companies themselves

**Get smart:**  The reforms will weaken industry groups’ traditional role as policy advocates, shifting them toward a more administrative and supervisory function.

**Get smarter:**  Tighter scrutiny over foreign exchanges and ideology could make participation more sensitive for foreign firms.
- This will make industry associations less useful as channels for policy insight and engagement.
