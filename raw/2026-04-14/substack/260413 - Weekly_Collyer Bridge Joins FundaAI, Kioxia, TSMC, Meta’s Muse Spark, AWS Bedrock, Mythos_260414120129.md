### Key Events Commentary
Meta’s new model - The latest model (Muse Spark) came in ahead of the market’s previously cautious expectations. On core dimensions such as multimodal understanding and general reasoning, it has effectively re-entered the top tier, with the gap to OpenAI and Google narrowing meaningfully. More importantly, this is not just a “model catch-up” story—it is a coordinated push across model, distribution, and product. Embedding these capabilities directly into Meta’s multi-billion-user ecosystem creates a structural advantage as AI shifts from raw capability to scaled usage. Market reaction reflects this reframing: the debate is moving from whether Meta can build competitive models to how effectively it can monetize AI at scale.
Looking forward, we remain constructive. Meta is clearly entering a new acceleration phase (org reset, stepped-up capex, next-gen models in flight), with a visible focus on strengthening coding and agentic capabilities — areas that align well with current scaling dynamics and data flywheels. If Meta can make progress here, its distribution advantage becomes even more powerful: model improvements can be rapidly productized into real user workflows, pushing AI from “chat” toward an execution layer embedded across its apps.
That said, two key variables still need to be monitored. First, how next-generation “productivity models” (especially coding and agentic workflows) will integrate with Meta’s core social/content surfaces — this will determine whether AI becomes a high-frequency utility or remains an add-on feature. Second, whether Meta leans further into enterprise business. While enterprise offers a larger monetization ceiling, it also introduces materially higher uncertainty around product definition, go-to-market, and competitive positioning. Ultimately, Meta is evolving from a model follower into a distribution-led AI platform player, but the slope from here will depend on how these two vectors play out.
AMZN CEO interview - We are in the process of previewing CSP’s Q1 results, but it’s likely that AWS could have a strong Q1. It’s not just about chip revenue — it’s more about the partnership model between AWS and Anthropic. Due to limited TPU availability, Claude Code has had to be far more aggressive than before in utilizing Trainium 2 through AWS Bedrock. The arrangement looks very similar to how Microsoft helps OpenAI sell APIs. We estimate AWS takes 70% of the revenue while bearing all the costs. In reality, the revenue from this sales-sharing arrangement would be larger than chip revenue alone. That said, we are very cautious about the AWS CEO’s claim that most training is done on TRN2. We believe only some post-training experiments have been completed on TRN2. In fact, more of the training has now shifted onto TPUs.
Anthropic Mythos and Claude Managed Agents - Although the announcements came back-to-back, they represent two distinct product lines and should be evaluated separately.
Mythos / Glasswing
CrowdStrike (CRWD) and Palo Alto Networks (PANW) were among the twelve launch partners granted prerelease access to Mythos. In the near term, this is likely a tangible positive because it allows them to harden their products earlier and integrate relevant capabilities into their own platforms ahead of broader adoption.
Over the medium to long term, however, the key question is whether Anthropic’s ongoing model iteration will begin to erode these vendors’ data moats and their ability to capture incremental security budget. The fact that a Mythos-class model can materially outperform on CyberGym without dedicated cybersecurity training suggests that reliance on large proprietary security datasets may be declining.
Cloudflare’s absence from the partner list could reinforce market perception that it remains behind the leading cyber vendors in both security product positioning and threat intelligence depth. That, in turn, adds pressure on Cloudflare’s narrative about strengthening its AI-era security capabilities.
Managed Agents
Anthropic is increasingly integrating the runtime layer and establishing a new platform layer, compressing the middle layer of the stack and challenging the broader SaaS moat narrative that many vendors have built around owning the orchestration layer.
While PANW and CRWD are still likely to be affected by software-sector beta, the pressure on them should be more limited for several reasons, including stronger compliance-oriented exposure, the strategic positioning conferred by Glasswing, and already established M&A activity and capability buildouts in agent security, including CyberArk, Seraphic, and SGNL.
For Cloudflare, Managed Agents pose a more direct challenge to its narrative around becoming a new deployment platform for the AI era.
That said, if MCP servers continue to be deployed on Workers, Cloudflare could still benefit as a tooling and infrastructure layer provider. However, that potential upside does not offset the pressure created by its exclusion from the Glasswing / Mythos ecosystem. These are parallel dynamics rather than opposing forces that can simply be netted against one another.
