Given that Gerstner owns these companies, I think he’s highlighting numbers that he knows are at least directionally accurate. Nonetheless, it is frustrating to follow these AI labs while they remain private companies and everyone’s job would be lot easier if both OpenAI and Anthropic became public companies. Since that is yet to be the case, our best bet to gauge the evolving economics of AI labs may be to study the publicly listed Chinese labs more closely. One such company is Knowledge Atlas Technology JSC which is more commonly known as “Zhipu AI” in China. Since going public early this year, the stock is up a cool ~6x in just three months! Despite such a meteoric rise, the Enterprise Value (EV) is still hovering around ~$50 Billion. Don’t be misled thinking Zhipu has an overly conservative shareholder base though; the stock currently trades at ~127x NTM revenue! But to their credit, revenue has become nearly 6x since 2023! Let’s take a bit deeper look into how they’re growing at such a rapid pace.
![image](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PrQg!,w_2400,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5019f5a6-224e-4061-9bb3-67cc84e017ec_2400x1240.png)
Zhipu discloses revenue in two broad categories: On-premise deployment and Cloud-based deployment.
On-premise deployment means the customer takes Zhipu’s model and runs it inside their own infrastructure. A bank, a telecom operator, a state-owned enterprise, or a government agency signs a contract, Zhipu's engineers show up, install the model weights and the serving stack on the customer's servers (or in the customer's private cloud), tune it for the customer's data and workloads, and hand it over. In China, this channel exists largely because data security, and regulatory comfort make many large customers unwilling to send sensitive data out to someone else's cloud.
In cloud-based deployment, as you can imagine, the customer does not install anything. They get an API key, they call Zhipu’s model over the internet, and Zhipu serves the tokens from its own GPU infrastructure. Revenue is recognized over time, either ratably for subscriptions or based on actual usage for pay-as-you-go . As you can see below, revenue from cloud-based deployment is basically tripling every year in each of the last three years.
![image](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4gvW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fcf7ecc13-6955-4eb2-9f5a-10a6decd507f_871x442.png)
In the most recent earnings, Zhipu also provided a separate segment disclosure of revenue by product line. Zhipu runs what it calls a MaaS platform, short for Model-as-a-Service, which is essentially the Chinese analogue to Anthropic’s or OpenAI’s API business. Customers can either call Zhipu's GLM models over the cloud and pay by usage, or they can have the models deployed on their own infrastructure for security and customization reasons, which Zhipu calls on-premise deployment. As you can see below, the API business is growing the fastest. It’s not just API business, the agents are also in very high demand in China. Notice the “enterprise agents” revenue below which increased by 249% YoY. The underlying offerings in enterprise agents include things like CoCo (their enterprise agent), AutoGLM (the general-purpose mobile agent), and increasingly the productized agent systems built on top of GLM for specific workflows. In practice, this line captures situations where the customer is not buying raw model access but rather a packaged agent that plans, uses tools, and completes tasks end-to-end. While the API business and agents were only ~30% of the overall revenue in 2024, they were almost half of the revenue in 2025.

If you look at Arena’s leaderboard , Zhipu’s model (GLM 5.1) is actually only behind Anthropic’s right now. At first glance, Zhipu’s overall gross margin appeared to be deteriorating substantially in 2025 despite revenue exploding. However, a closer look reveals a changing economics underneath. Even though overall gross margin went down from 56.3% in 2024 to 41.0% in 2025, this contraction was fully explained both by margin shift to lower margin and but higher growth cloud based deployment AND falling margin of on-prem deployment. On-Prem margins tend to be fundamentally a bit lumpy. Management's explanation is that meeting accelerating customer demand required investing more in delivery resources, which usually means hiring solutions engineers and absorbing project costs to ship contracts on time. It won’t surprise me if the margins stabilize and improve over time.
The margin trajectory of cloud-based deployment segment is far more interesting. Notice how gross margin here was precipitously falling and even turned negative in 1H’25 when the Chinese labs were essentially on a death march to grab market share. However, as mentioned earlier, once Zhipu realized they can actually exercise pricing power here, gross margin transformed from negative in 1H’25 to 22.4% in 2H’25. Given their further price increases in 2026 and removing discounts, I think it’s highly likely that gross margin will continue to increase in this segment in 2026. Looking at this trajectory gives credence to what Brad Gerstner mentioned in All-in episode. Indeed, margin went from negative in 1H’25 to increasing at a rapid pace every month now as the AI labs are capitalizing on the massive demand.

Like AI labs in the US, Chinese labs also don’t consider the model training costs in their gross margin although as I highlighted earlier, most of these revenue can potentially disappear if one lab trains a better model while the other one sits out which make model training costs to me quite intertwined with their revenue sustainability. AI labs bulls argue that regardless of how you define gross margin, AI labs should be able to gain a lot of leverage over the model training costs over time as their revenue becomes larger and larger while model training costs grow substantially slower than topline. Indeed, Zhipu’s financials gives some early credence to AI labs bulls’ arguments. As you can see below, Zhipu’s R&D costs were flat in 1H vs 2H’25 despite revenue becoming ~2.8x in 2H’25 vs 1H’25.  The numbers still look ugly as R&D costs are still ~3x of their revenue in 2H’25 but if you squint, you can see how the math can work at scale. It’s not a game for the faint hearted, of course, but at a sufficiently large scale, the math can start to make sense.

One other benefit for the labs is basically the entire world is doing the job of marketing for them. Zhipu’s S&M costs actually declined in 2H’25 (vs 1H’25) while revenue was exploding. You don’t need to promote your product when your customers are suffering from “compute panic”.

Of course, given the valuation, there are plenty of believers that the economics will work more than fine for these labs. While I incrementally updated my opinions on AI labs economics positively after digging into Zhipu’s financials, we still have a long road ahead to determine whether these businesses can go from negative 400% operating margin to ~15-30% operating margin at scale. It’s possible, but I keep wondering how competition will hit up once we enter more normalized demand-supply compute environment. You may think such demand won’t be closing anytime soon, but given the valuation people are paying, even if compute demand-supply normalized in 2030, the impact on the stocks could be sobering. If you are incredulous, imagine being in 2021 and drooling over software companies’ revenue growth trajectory and yet today not only buyers of these stocks are absconding at an accelerating pace but also many of them are trading at 2018-19 prices when their revenue was a fraction of today’s level. It’s easy to be infatuated with revenue, but the weight of economics comes for everyone. These questions will come for AI labs as well. Nonetheless, we have not seen revenue growth trajectory at this scale ever, especially at Anthropic and OpenAI’s scale. So, it is certainly in the realm of possibility that the AI labs may prove the naysayers wrong as they march towards utter dominance in the broader technology industry and beyond.
